

[[sec_5]]
== Basic principles

[[sec_5.1]]
=== General

[[sec_5.1.1]]
==== {blank}
(((input quantity)))
(((measurement function,univariate)))
(((output quantity)))
(((quantity,real vector)))

In the GUM [<<JCGM_100_2008,clause=4.1>>], a measuring system is modelled
in terms of a function involving real input quantities
stem:[X_1, ..., X_N] and a real output quantity stem:[Y] in the form
of <<eq_1>>, namely stem:[Y = f(bb X)], where
stem:[bb X -= (X_1, ..., X_N)^{sf(T)}] is referred to as the real
vector input quantity. This function is known as a real univariate
measurement function (see <<note_3.9.1>>).

[[sec_5.1.2]]
==== {blank}
(((output quantity)))
(((quantity,real vector)))

In practice, not all measuring systems encountered can be modelled
as a measurement function in a single scalar output quantity. Such
systems might instead involve either

. a number of output quantities stem:[Y_1, ..., Y_m] (denoted collectively
by the real vector output quantity stem:[Y -= (Y_1, ..., Y_m)^{sf(T)})],
taking the form <<eq_1,droploc%>>, namely stem:[bb Y = bb f(bb X)],
or
. the more general form of measurement model, taking the form <<eq_2,droploc%>>,
namely stem:[bb h(bb Y,bb X) = bb 0].

[[sec_5.1.3]]
==== {blank}
(((law of propagation of uncertainty)))
(((measurement model,complex)))
(((quantity,complex)))

Further, some or all of the components of stem:[bb X] and
stem:[bb Y] might correspond to components (real part and imaginary
part, or magnitude and phase) of complex input quantities. Therefore,
all measurement models can be considered (without loss of generality)
as real. However, a treatment that is simpler than the corresponding
treatment involving real and imaginary parts applies for the complex
case <<ref14>>. The treatment gives succinct matrix expressions for
the law of propagation of uncertainty when applied to measurement
models with complex quantities (complex models). See <<sec_6.4>> and
<<annexA>>.

[[sec_5.1.4]]
==== {blank}

This GUM Supplement considers the more general measurement models
in <<sec_5.1.2>> and <<sec_5.1.3>>.

[[sec_5.2]]
=== Main stages of uncertainty evaluation
(((stages of uncertainty evaluation)))

[[sec_5.2.1]]
==== {blank}
(((covariance matrix)))
(((coverage probability)))
(((coverage region)))
(((expectation)))
(((input quantity)))
(((input quantity,assigning a probability density function (PDF) to)))
(((measurand)))
(((measurement function)))
(((output quantity)))
(((output quantity,probability density function (PDF) for)))
(((stages of uncertainty evaluation,formulation)))
(((stages of uncertainty evaluation,propagation)))
(((stages of uncertainty evaluation,summarizing)))
(((vector measurand)))

The main stages of uncertainty evaluation constitute formulation,
propagation, and summarizing:

. [[item_5.2.1_a]]_Formulation_:
.. define the output quantity stem:[bb Y], the quantity intended to
be measured (the vector measurand),
.. determine the input quantity stem:[bb X] upon which stem:[bb Y]
depends,
.. develop a measurement function [stem:[bb f] in <<eq_1,droploc%>>]
or measurement model <<eq_2,droploc%>> relating stem:[bb X] and
stem:[bb Y],
.. on the basis of available knowledge assign PDFs -- Gaussian (normal),
rectangular (uniform), etc. -- to the components of stem:[bb X]. Assign
instead a joint PDF to the components of stem:[bb X] that are not
pairwise independent,
. [[item_5.2.1_b]]_Propagation_:
+
--
propagate the PDFs for the components of stem:[bb X] through the model
to obtain the (joint) PDF for stem:[bb Y],
--
. [[item_5.2.1_c]]_Summarizing_:
+
--
use the PDF for stem:[bb Y] to obtain
--
.. the expectation of stem:[bb Y], taken as an estimate stem:[bb y]
of stem:[bb Y],
.. the covariance matrix of stem:[bb Y], taken as the covariance matrix
stem:[bb(U_y)] associated with stem:[bb y], and
.. a coverage region containing stem:[Y] with a specified probability
stem:[p] (the coverage probability).

[[sec_5.2.2]]
==== {blank}
(((coverage probability)))
(((input quantity,assigning a probability density function (PDF) to)))

The steps in the formulation stage are carried out by the metrologist.
Guidance on the assignment of PDFs (step 4 of stage <<item_5.2.1_a>>)
is given in GUM Supplement 1 for some common cases and in <<sec_5.3>>.
The propagation and summarizing stages, <<item_5.2.1_b>> and <<item_5.2.1_c>>,
for which detailed guidance is provided here, require no further metrological
information, and in principle can be carried out to any required numerical
tolerance for the problem specified in the formulation stage.

NOTE: Once the formulation stage <<item_5.2.1_a>> has been carried
out, the PDF for stem:[bb Y] is completely specified mathematically,
but generally the calculation of the expectation, covariance matrix
and coverage regions require numerical methods that involve a degree
of approximation.

[[sec_5.3]]
=== Probability density functions for the input quantities

[[sec_5.3.1]]
==== General
(((Gaussian distribution,multivariate)))
(((indication values)))
(((input quantity,assigning a probability density function (PDF) to)))
(((t-distribution,multivariate)))

GUM Supplement 1 gives guidance on the assignment, in some common
circumstances, of PDFs to the input quantities stem:[X_i] in the formulation
stage of uncertainty evaluation [<<JCGM_101_2008,clause=6>>]. The
only multivariate distribution considered in GUM Supplement 1 is the
multivariate Gaussian [<<JCGM_101_2008,clause=6.4.8>>]. This distribution
is assigned to the input quantity stem:[bb X] when the estimate
stem:[bb x] and associated covariance matrix stem:[bb(U_x)] constitute
the only information available about stem:[bb X]. A further multivariate
distribution, the multivariate _t_-distribution, is described in <<sec_5.3.2>>.
This distribution arises when a series of indication values, regarded
as being obtained independently from a vector quantity with unknown
expectation and unknown covariance matrix having a multivariate Gaussian
distribution, is the only information available about stem:[bb X].
Also see <<sec_6.5.4>>.

[[sec_5.3.2]]
==== Multivariate _t_-distribution
(((t-distribution,multivariate)))

[[sec_5.3.2.1]]
===== {blank}
(((Bayes' theorem)))
(((Gaussian distribution,multivariate)))
(((indication values)))

Suppose that a series of stem:[n] indication values
stem:[x_1, ..., x_n], each of dimension stem:[N xx 1], is available,
with stem:[n > N], regarded as being obtained independently from a
quantity with unknown expectation stem:[bb mu] and covariance matrix
stem:[sum] of dimension stem:[N xx N] having the multivariate Gaussian
distribution stem:[N(bb mu, bb sum)]. The desired input quantity
stem:[bb X] of dimension stem:[N xx 1] is taken to be equal to
stem:[bb mu]. Then, assigning a non-informative joint prior distribution
to stem:[bb mu] and stem:[bb sum], and using Bayes' theorem, the marginal
(joint) PDF for stem:[bb X] is a multivariate _t_-distribution
stem:[t_nu(bb(bar x), bb S//n)] with stem:[nu = n - N] degrees of
freedom <<ref11>>, where 

[stem%unnumbered]
++++
bar x = 1/n (x_1 + ... + x_n), " " " " bb(S) = 1/nu [(x_1 - bar x)
(x_1 - bar x)^{sf T} + ... + (x_n - bar x)(x_n - bar x)^{sf T}].
++++

NOTE: The prior can be assigned in other ways, which would influence
the degrees of freedom or even the distribution.

[[sec_5.3.2.2]]
===== {blank}

The PDF for stem:[bb X] is

[stem%unnumbered]
++++
g_{bb(X)}(bb(xi)) = {Gamma(n//2)}/{Gamma (nu//2)(pi nu)^{N//2}} xx
["det"(bb S//n)]^{-1//2} [1 + 1/nu (bb xi - bar(bb x))^{sf T}
(bb S/n)^{-1} (bb xi - bar (bb x))]^{-n//2}
++++

where stem:[Gamma(z)] is the gamma function with argument stem:[z].

[[sec_5.3.2.3]]
===== {blank}

stem:[bb X] has expectation and covariance matrix

[stem%unnumbered]
++++
bb E(bb X) = bar(bb x), " " " " bb V(bb X) = nu/{nu - 2} bb(S)/n,
++++

where stem:[bb E(bb X)] is defined only for stem:[nu > 1] (that is
for stem:[n > N + 1]) and stem:[bb V(bb X)] only for stem:[nu > 2]
(that is for stem:[n > N+2]).

[[sec_5.3.2.4]]
===== {blank}
(((Cholesky decomposition)))
(((Gaussian distribution)))

To make a random draw from stem:[t_{nu}(bar(bb(x)),bb S//n)], make
stem:[N] random draws stem:[z_i], stem:[i = 1, ..., N], from the standard
Gaussian distribution N(0, 1) and a single random draw stem:[w] from
stem:[sf(X)_{nu}^2], the chi-squared distribution with nu degrees
of freedom, and form

[stem%unnumbered]
++++
bb (xi = bar bb(x) + L z) sqrt(nu/w), " " " " z = (z_1, ..., z_N)^{sf(T)},
++++

where stem:[bb L] is the lower triangular matrix of dimension
stem:[N xx N] given by the Cholesky decomposition
stem:[bb(S)//n = bb(LL)^{sf T}] <<ref13>>.

NOTE: The matrix stem:[bb L] can be determined as in reference <<ref13>>,
for example.

[[sec_5.3.3]]
==== Construction of multivariate probability density functions
(((copula)))

When the input quantities stem:[X_1, ..., X_N] are correlated, the
information that typically is available about them are the forms of
their PDFs (say, that one is Gaussian, another is rectangular, etc.),
estimates stem:[x_1, ..., x_N], used as their expectations, associated
standard uncertainties stem:[u(x_1), ..., u(x_N)], used as their standard
deviations, and covariances associated with pairs of the stem:[x_i].
A mathematical device known as a copula <<ref20>> can be used to produce
a PDF for stem:[bb X] consistent with such information. Such a PDF
is not unique.

[[sec_5.4]]
=== Propagation of distributions
(((propagation of distributions)))

[[sec_5.4.1]]
==== {blank}

<<figure1>> (left) shows an instance of a measurement model in which
there are stem:[N = 3] mutually independent input quantities
stem:[bb X = (X_1, X_2, X_3)^{sf(T)}] and stem:[m = 2] output quantities
stem:[bb Y = (Y_1, Y_2)^{sf(T)}]. The measurement function is
stem:[bb f = (f_1, f_2)^{sf(T)}]. For stem:[i = 1, 2, 3], stem:[X_i]
is assigned the PDF stem:[g_{X_i}(xi_i)], and stem:[bb Y] is characterized
by the joint PDF stem:[g_{bb Y}(eta) -= g_{Y_1, Y_2} (eta_1, eta_2)].
<<figure1>> (right) adapts this instance to where stem:[X_1] and stem:[X_2]
are mutually dependent and characterized by the joint PDF
stem:[g_{X_1,X_2} (xi_1, xi_2)].

[[sec_5.4.2]]
==== {blank}
index-range:multistage[(((measurement model,multistage)))]

There might be an additional output quantity, stem:[bb Q] say, depending
on stem:[bb Y]. stem:[bb Y] would be regarded as an input quantity
to a further measurement model represented in terms of a measurement
function stem:[bb t], say:

[stem%unnumbered]
++++
bb (Q = t(Y)).
++++

For example, stem:[bb Y] might be the vector of masses in a set of
mass standards, and stem:[bb Q] the sums of some of them.

[[figure1]]
.Propagation of distributions for stem:[N = 3] input quantities and stem:[m = 2] output quantities when the input quantities stem:[X_1, X_2] and stem:[X_3] are mutually independent and (right) when stem:[X_1] and stem:[X_2] are mutually dependent (<<sec_5.4.1>>)

[[sec_5.4.3]]
==== {blank}

Composition of the measurement functions stem:[bb f] and
stem:[bb t], which are regarded as sub-models, gives stem:[bb Q] as
a function of stem:[bb X]. It can, however, be desirable to retain
the individual sub-models when they relate to functionally distinct
stages. These two sub-models constitute an instance of a multistage
model (see <<sec_3.12>>).

[[sec_5.4.4]]
==== {blank}


The case when the final stage of a multistage model has an output
quantity that is a single scalar quantity, and that quantity is the
only output quantity of interest, can be handled using GUM Supplement
1.[[multistage]]

[[sec_5.5]]
=== Obtaining summary information

[[sec_5.5.1]]
==== {blank}
(((estimate of input quantities)))
(((expectation)))

An estimate stem:[bb y] of the output quantity stem:[bb Y] is the
expectation stem:[bb (E(Y))]. The covariance matrix stem:[bb(U_y)]
associated with stem:[bb y] is the covariance matrix stem:[bb(V(Y))].

[[sec_5.5.2]]
==== {blank}
(((coverage region)))
(((random variable)))

For a coverage probability stem:[p], a coverage region
stem:[R_{bb Y}] for stem:[bb Y] satisfies

[stem%unnumbered]
++++
int_{R_{bb Y}} g_{bb Y} (bb eta) "d" bb(eta) = p
++++

NOTE: Although random variables corresponding to some quantities might
be characterized by distributions having no expectation or covariance
matrix (see, for example, <<sec_5.3.2>>), a coverage region for
stem:[bb Y] always exists.

NOTE: Generally there is more than one coverage region for a stated
coverage probability stem:[p].

[[sec_5.5.3]]
==== {blank}
(((coverage interval,probabilistically symmetric)))
(((coverage interval,shortest)))
(((coverage region,smallest)))

There is no direct multivariate counterpart of the probabilistically
symmetric stem:[100p%] coverage interval considered in GUM Supplement
1. There is, however, a counterpart of the shortest stem:[100p%] coverage
interval. It is the stem:[100p %] coverage region for stem:[bb Y]
of smallest hyper-volume.

[[sec_5.6]]
=== Implementations of the propagation of distributions
(((propagation of distributions,implementations of)))

[[sec_5.6.1]]
==== {blank}
(((law of propagation of uncertainty)))
(((Monte Carlo method (MCM))))
(((propagation of distributions,analytical methods for)))
(((propagation of distributions,numerical methods for)))
(((propagation of uncertainty)))
(((stages of uncertainty evaluation,propagation)))

The propagation of distributions can be implemented in several ways:

. analytical methods, that is methods that provide a mathematical
representation of the PDF for stem:[bb Y],
. uncertainty propagation based on replacing the model by a first-order
Taylor series approximation (a generalization of the treatment in
the GUM [<<JCGM_100_2008,clause=5.1.2>>]) -- the (generalized) law
of propagation of uncertainty,
. numerical methods [<<JCGM_100_2008,clause=G.1.5>>] that implement
the propagation of distributions, specifically using a Monte Carlo
method (MCM).

NOTE: Solutions expressible analytically are ideal in that they do
not introduce any approximation. They are applicable in simple cases
only, however. These methods are not considered further in this Supplement,
apart from in the examples for comparison purposes.

NOTE: MCM as considered here is regarded as a means for providing
a numerical representation of the distribution for the vector output
quantity, rather than a simulation method per se. In the context of
the propagation stage of uncertainty evaluation, the problem to be
solved is deterministic, there being no random physical process to
be simulated.

[[sec_5.6.2]]
==== {blank}
(((propagation of uncertainty)))

In uncertainty propagation, the estimate stem:[bb(x = E(X))] of
stem:[bb X] and the associated covariance matrix stem:[bb(U_x) = bb(V(X))]
are propagated through (a linearization of) the measurement model.
This Supplement provides procedures for doing so for the various types
of model considered.

==== {blank}
(((law of propagation of uncertainty)))

stem:[bb X] is estimated by stem:[bb x = (x_1, x_2, x_3)^{sf(T)}]

[[sec_5.6.4]]
==== {blank}
(((Monte Carlo method (MCM))))

In MCM, a discrete representation of the (joint) probability distribution
for stem:[bb X] is propagated through the measurement model to obtain
a discrete representation of the (joint) probability distribution
for stem:[bb Y] from which the required summary information is determined.
