
[[sec_6]]
== GUM uncertainty framework
(((GUM uncertainty framework)))

[[sec_6.1]]
=== General

[[sec_6.1.1]]
==== {blank}
(((propagation of uncertainty)))

The propagation of uncertainty for measurement models that are more
general than the form stem:[Y = f(bb X)]
in the GUM is described (see <<sec_6.2>> and <<sec_6.3>>). Although
such measurement models are not directly considered in the GUM, the
same underlying principles may be used to propagate estimates of the
input quantities and the uncertainties associated with the estimates
through the measurement model to obtain estimates of the output quantities
and the associated uncertainties. Mathematical expressions for the
evaluation of uncertainty are stated using matrix-vector notation,
rather than the subscripted summations given in the GUM, because generally
such expressions are more compact and more naturally implemented within
modern software packages and computer languages.

[[sec_6.1.2]]
==== {blank}
(((law of propagation of uncertainty)))

For the application of the law of propagation of uncertainty, the
same information concerning the input quantities as for the univariate
measurement model treated in the GUM is used:

. an estimate stem:[bb x = (x_1, ..., x_N)^{sf(T)}] of the input quantity
stem:[bb X],
. the covariance matrix stem:[bb(U_x)] associated with stem:[bb x]
containing the covariances stem:[u(xi, x_j)], stem:[i = 1, ..., N],
stem:[j = 1, ..., N], associated with stem:[x_i] and stem:[x_j].

[[sec_6.1.3]]
==== {blank}

The description of the propagation of uncertainty given in <<sec_6.2>>
and <<sec_6.3>> is for real measurement models, including complex
measurement models that are expressed in terms of real quantities.
A treatment for complex measurement models is given in <<sec_6.4>>.
Also see <<sec_5.1.3>>.

[[sec_6.1.4]]
==== {blank}

Obtaining a coverage region for a vector output quantity is described
in <<sec_6.5>>.

[[sec_6.2]]
=== Propagation of uncertainty for explicit multivariate measurement models
(((measurement model,explicit multivariate)))
index-range:uncertainty[(((propagation of uncertainty)))]

[[sec_6.2.1]]
==== General

[[sec_6.2.1.1]]
===== {blank}
(((measurement function,multivariate)))

An explicit multivariate measurement model specifies a relationship
between an output quantity stem:[bb Y = (Y_1, ..., Y_m)^{sf(T)}] and
an input quantity stem:[bb X = (X_1, ..., X_N)^{sf(T)}], and takes
the form

[stem%unnumbered]
++++
bb Y = bb(f(X)), " " " " bb f = (f_1, ..., f_m)^{sf(T)},
++++

where stem:[bb f] denotes the multivariate measurement function.

NOTE: Any particular function stem:[f_j(bb X)] may depend only on
a subset of stem:[bb X], with each stem:[X_i] appearing in at least
one function.

[[sec_6.2.1.2]]
===== {blank}
(((estimate of input quantities,from GUM uncertainty framework)))

Given an estimate stem:[bb x] of stem:[bb X], an estimate of
stem:[bb Y] is

[stem%unnumbered]
++++
bb(y = f(x)).
++++

[[sec_6.2.1.3]]
===== {blank}
(((covariance matrix,from GUM uncertainty framework)))
(((sensitivity matrix)))

The covariance matrix of dimension stem:[m xx m] associated with
stem:[bb y] is

[stem%unnumbered]
++++
bb(U_y) =
[(u(y_1, y_1), cdots, u(y_1, y_m)),
(vdots,ddots,vdots)
(u(y_m, y_1), cdots, u(y_m, y_m))],
++++

where stem:["cov"(y_j, y_j) = u^2(y_j)], and is given by

[[eq_3]]
[stem]
++++
bb(U_y) = bb(C_x U_x C_x^{sf T}),
++++

where stem:[bb(C_x)] is the sensitivity matrix of dimension
stem:[m xx N] given by evaluating

[stem%unnumbered]
++++
bb(C_X) =
[({del f_1}/{del X_1}, cdots, {del f_1}/{del X_N})
(vdots,ddots,vdots)
({del f_m}/{del X_1}, cdots, {del f_m}/{del X_N})]
++++

at stem:[bb X = bb x] <<ref19,page=29>>.

[[ex_6.2.2_1]]
.Resistance and reactance of a circuit element [<<JCGM_100_2008,clause=H.2>>]
====
The resistance stem:[R] and reactance stem:[X] of a circuit element
are determined by measuring the amplitude stem:[V] of a sinusoidal
alternating potential difference applied to it, the amplitude stem:[I]
of the alternating current passed through it, and the phase angle
stem:[phi] between the two. The bivariate measurement model for stem:[R]
and stem:[X] in terms of stem:[V], stem:[I] and stem:[phi] is

[[eq_4]]
[stem]
++++
R = f_1(V, I, phi) = V/I cos phi, " " " " X = f_2(V, I, phi) = V/I sin phi .
++++

In terms of the general notation, stem:[N = 3], stem:[m = 2],
stem:[bb X -= (V, I, phi)^{sf(T)}] and stem:[bb Y -= (R, X)^{sf(T)}].

An estimate stem:[bb y -= (hat R, hat X)^{sf(T)}] of resistance and
reactance is obtained by evaluating expressions <<eq_4,droploc%>>
at an estimate stem:[x -= (hat V, hat I, hat phi)^{sf(T)}] of the
input quantity stem:[bb X].

The covariance matrix stem:[bb(U_y)] of dimension stem:[2 xx 2] associated
with stem:[bb y] is given by <<eq_3>>, where stem:[bb(C_x)] is the
sensitivity matrix of dimension stem:[2 xx 3] given by evaluating

[stem%unnumbered]
++++
[({del f_1}/{del V}, {del f_1}/{del I}, {del f_1}/{del phi}),({del f_2}/{del V}, {del f_2}/{del I}, {del f_2}/{del phi})] =
[({cos phi}/I, -{V cos phi}/{I^2}, -{V sin phi}/I),({sin phi}/I, -{V sin phi}/{I^2}, -{V cos phi}/I)]
++++

at stem:[bb X = bb x], and stem:[bb(U_x)] is the covariance matrix
of dimension stem:[3 xx 3] associated with stem:[bb x].

NOTE: In the GUM, reactance is denoted by stem:[X], which is the notation
used here. The reactance stem:[X], a component of the vector output
quantity stem:[bb Y], is not to be confused with stem:[bb X], the
vector input quantity.
====

[[ex_6.2.2_2]]
.Reflection coefficient measured by a microwave reflectometer (approach 1)
====
The (complex) reflection coefficient stem:[bb(Gamma)] measured by
a calibrated microwave reflectometer, such as an automatic network
analyser, is given by the complex measurement model

[[eq_5]]
[stem]
++++
bb Gamma = {bb(a W + b)}/{bb(c W) + 1}
++++

where stem:[bb W] is the (complex) uncorrected reflection coefficient
and stem:[bb a], stem:[bb b] and stem:[bb c] are (complex) calibration
coefficients characterizing the reflectometer <<ref10>>, <<ref16>>,
<<ref26>>.

In terms of the general notation, and working with real and imaginary
parts of the quantities involved, stem:[N = 8], stem:[m = 2],

[stem%unnumbered]
++++
bb X -= (a_R, a_I, b_R, b_I, c_R, c_I, W_R, W_I)^{sf(T)}" " "and" " " bb Y -= (Gamma_R, Gamma_I)^{sf(T)}.
++++

An estimate stem:[bb y -= (hat(Gamma)_R, hat(Gamma)_I)^{sf(T)}] of
the (complex) reflection coefficient is given by the real and imaginary
parts of the right-hand side of <<eq_5>> evaluated at the estimate
stem:[bb x] of the input quantity stem:[bb X].

The covariance matrix stem:[bb(U_y)] of dimension stem:[2 xx 2] associated
with stem:[bb y] is given by <<eq_3>>, where stem:[bb(C_x)] is the
sensitivity matrix of dimension stem:[2 xx 8] given by evaluating

[stem%unnumbered]
++++
[({del Gamma_R}/{del a_R}, {del Gamma_R}/{del a_I}, {del Gamma_R}/{del b_R}, {del Gamma_R}/{del b_I}, {del Gamma_R}/{del c_R}, {del Gamma_R}/{del c_I}, {del Gamma_R}/{del W_R}, {del Gamma_R}/{del W_I}),
({del Gamma_I}/{del a_R}, {del Gamma_I}/{del a_I}, {del Gamma_I}/{del b_R}, {del Gamma_I}/{del b_I}, {del Gamma_I}/{del c_R}, {del Gamma_I}/{del c_I}, {del Gamma_I}/{del W_R}, {del Gamma_I}/{del W_I})]
++++

at stem:[bb X = bb x], and stem:[bb(U_x)] is the covariance matrix
of dimension stem:[8 xx 8] associated with stem:[bb x].
====

.Calibration of mass standards
====
This example constitutes an instance of a multistage model (see <<sec_3.12>>,
<<sec_5.4.2>> and <<sec_5.4.3>>).

A set of stem:[q] mass standards of unknown mass values
stem:[bb m = (m_1, ..., m_q)^{sf(T)}] is calibrated by comparison
with a reference kilogram, using a mass comparator, a sensitivity
weight for determining the comparator sensitivity, and a number of
ancillary instruments such as a thermometer, a barometer and a hygrometer
for determining the correction due to air buoyancy. The reference
kilogram and the sensitivity weight have masses stem:[m_R] and stem:[m_S],
respectively. The calibration is carried out by performing, according
to a suitable measurement procedure, a sufficient number stem:[k]
of comparisons between groups of standards, yielding apparent, namely,
in-air differences stem:[bb delta = (delta_1, ..., delta_k)^{sf(T)}].
Corresponding buoyancy corrections stem:[bb b = (b_1, ..., b_k)^{sf(T)}]
are calculated. In-vacuo mass differences stem:[bb X] are obtained
from the sub-model stem:[bb(X = f (W))], where
stem:[bb W = (m_R, m_S, bb(delta)^{sf T}, bb(b)^{sf T})^{sf T}].

An estimate stem:[bb y -= ( hat(m)_1, ..., hat(m)_q)^{sf(T)}] of the
masses stem:[bb m] is typically given by the least-squares solution
of the over-determined system of equations stem:[bb(Am = X)], where
stem:[bb A] is a matrix of dimension stem:[k xx q] with elements equal
to stem:[+1], stem:[-1] or zero, according to the mass standards involved
in each comparison, and respecting the uncertainties associated with
the estimate stem:[bb x] of stem:[bb X]. With this choice, the estimate
stem:[bb y] is given by

[[eq_6]]
[stem]
++++
bb(y) = bb(U_y A^{sf T} U_x x),
++++

where the covariance matrix stem:[bb(U_y)] of dimension
stem:[q xx q] associated with stem:[bb y] is given by
stem:[bb(U_y) = (bb(A^{sf T} U_x^{-1} A))^{-1}] is the covariance
matrix of dimension stem:[k xx k] associated with stem:[bb x]. A more
detailed description of the sub-model, as well as a procedure for
obtaining stem:[bb(U_x)] in terms of stem:[bb(U_w)], the covariance
matrix associated with the estimate stem:[bb w] of stem:[bb W], is
available <<ref3>>.

The multivariate measurement model for this example can be expressed
as

[stem%unnumbered]
++++
bb Y = bb(U_y A^{sf T} U_x^{-1} X),
++++

where the measurement function is stem:[bb(U_y A^{sf T} U_x^{-1} X)].
In terms of the general notation, stem:[N = k], stem:[m = q] and
stem:[bb(Y -= m)].

NOTE: It is preferable computationally to obtain the estimate given
by formula <<eq_6,droploc%>> by an algorithm based on orthogonal factorization
<<ref13>>, rather than use this explicit formula.

[[sec_6.3]]
=== Propagation of uncertainty for implicit multivariate measurement models
(((measurement model,implicit multivariate)))

[[sec_6.3.1]]
==== General

[[sec_6.3.1.1]]
===== {blank}

An implicit multivariate measurement model specifies a relationship
between an output quantity stem:[bb Y = (Y_1, ..., Y_m)^{sf(T)}] and
an input quantity stem:[bb X = (X_1, ..., X_N)^{sf(T)}], and takes
the form

[stem%unnumbered]
++++
bb(h(Y, X)) = 0, " " " " bb(h) = (h_1, ..., h_m)^{sf(T)}.
++++

[[sec_6.3.1.2]]
===== {blank}
(((estimate of input quantities,from GUM uncertainty framework)))

Given an estimate stem:[bb x] of stem:[bb X], an estimate
stem:[bb y] of stem:[bb Y] is given by the solution of the system
of equations

[[eq_7]]
[stem]
++++
bb(h(y, x) = 0).
++++

NOTE: The system of equations <<eq_7,droploc%>> has generally to be
solved numerically for stem:[bb y], using, for example, Newton's method
<<ref12>> or a variant of that method, starting from an approximation
stem:[y^{(0)}] to the solution.

[[sec_6.3.1.3]]
===== {blank}
(((covariance matrix,from GUM uncertainty framework)))
(((sensitivity matrix)))

The covariance matrix stem:[bb(U_y)] of dimension stem:[m xx m] associated
with stem:[bb y] is evaluated from the system of equations

[[eq_8]]
[stem]
++++
bb(C_y U_y C_y^{sf T} = C_x U_x C_x^{sf T}),
++++

where stem:[bb(C_y)] is the sensitivity matrix of dimension
stem:[m xx m] containing the partial derivatives
stem:[del h_{ell}//del Y_j], stem:[ell = 1, ..., m],
stem:[j = 1, ..., m], and stem:[bb(C_x)] is the sensitivity matrix
of dimension stem:[m xx N] containing the partial derivatives
stem:[del h_{ell}//del X_i], stem:[ell = 1, ..., m],
stem:[i = 1, ..., N], all derivatives being evaluated at
stem:[bb X = bb x] and stem:[bb Y = bb y].

NOTE: The covariance matrix stem:[bb(U_y)] in <<eq_8>> is not defined
if stem:[bb(C_y)] is singular.

NOTE: <<eq_8>> is obtained in a similar way as <<eq_3>>, with the
use of the implicit function theorem.

[[sec_6.3.1.4]]
===== {blank}
(((covariance matrix)))
(((sensitivity coefficient)))
(((sensitivity matrix)))

Formally, the covariance matrices stem:[bb(U_x)] and stem:[bb(U_y)]
are related by

[[eq_9]]
[stem]
++++
bb(U_y) = bb(C U_x C^{sf T})
++++

where

[[eq_10]]
[stem]
++++
bb(C) = bb(C_y^{-1} C_x),
++++

a matrix of sensitivity coefficients of dimension stem:[m xx N].

[[sec_6.3.1.5]]
===== {blank}

<<annexB>> contains a procedure for forming stem:[bb(U_y)]. It is
not recommended that stem:[bb(U_y)] is obtained directly by evaluating
<<eq_10>> and then <<eq_9>>, such a procedure is less stable numerically.

[[sec_6.3.2]]
==== Examples
(((GUM uncertainty framework,examples of)))

[example]
.Set of pressures generated by a pressure balance
====
The pressure stem:[p] generated by a pressure balance is defined implicitly
by the equation

[[eq_11]]
[stem]
++++
p = {m_w (1 - rho_a//rho_w) g_{ell}}/{A_0 (1 + lambda_p)(1 + alpha delta theta)}, 
++++

where stem:[m_w] is the total applied mass, stem:[rho_a] and stem:[rho_w]
are, respectively, the densities of air and the applied masses, stem:[g_{ell}]
is the local acceleration due to gravity, stem:[A_0] is the effective
cross-sectional area of the balance at zero pressure, stem:[lambda]
is the distortion coefficient of the piston-cylinder assembly, stem:[alpha]
is the coefficient of thermal expansion, and stem:[delta theta] is
the deviation from a 20 °C reference Celsius temperature <<ref17>>.

Let stem:[p_1, ..., p_q] denote the generated pressures for, respectively,
applied masses stem:[m_{w, 1}, ..., m_{w, q}] and temperature deviations
stem:[delta theta_1, ..., delta theta_q].

In terms of the general notation, stem:[N = 6 + 2q], stem:[m = q],
stem:[bb X -= (A_0, lambda, alpha, delta theta_1, m_{w, 1}, ..., delta theta_q, m_{w, q}, rho_a, rho_w, g_{ell})^{sf(T)}] and
stem:[Y -= (p_1, ..., p_q)^{sf(T)}].

stem:[bb X] and stem:[bb Y] are related by the measurement model

[[eq_12]]
[stem]
++++
h_j(bb Y,bb X) = A_0 p_j (1 + lambda p_j) (1 + alpha delta theta_j) - m_{w, j} (1 - rho_a//rho_w) g_{ell} = 0, " " " " j = 1, ..., q .
++++

An estimate stem:[hat(p)_j] of stem:[hat p_j] is obtained by solving
an equation of the form <<eq_12,droploc%>> given estimates of
stem:[A_0], stem:[lambda], stem:[alpha], stem:[delta theta_j],
stem:[m_{w, j}], stem:[rho_a], stem:[rho_w] and stem:[g_{ell}]. However,
the resulting estimates have associated covariances because they all
depend on the measured quantities stem:[A_0], stem:[lambda], stem:[alpha],
stem:[rho_a], stem:[rho_w] and stem:[g_{ell}].

The covariance matrix stem:[bb(U_y)] of dimension stem:[q xx q] associated
with stem:[y -= (hat p_1, ..., hat p_q)^{sf(T)}] is evaluated from
<<eq_8>>, where stem:[bb(C_y)] is the sensitivity matrix of dimension
stem:[q xx q] containing the partial derivatives del
stem:[h_{ell}//del y_j], stem:[ell = 1, ..., q],
stem:[j = 1, ..., q], and stem:[bb(C_x)]
is the matrix of dimension stem:[q xx (6 + 2q)] containing the partial
derivatives del stem:[h_{ell}//del X_i], stem:[ell = 1, ..., q],
stem:[i = 1, ..., 6 + 2q], both evaluated at stem:[bb(X = x)] and
stem:[bb(Y = y)], and stem:[bb(U_x)] is the covariance matrix of dimension
stem:[(6 + 2q) xx (6 + 2q)] associated with stem:[bb x].

NOTE: A measurement function [giving stem:[Y_j (-= p_j)] explicitly
as a function of stem:[bb X]] can be determined in this case as the
solution of a quadratic equation. Such a form is not necessarily numerically
stable. Moreover, measurement models involving additional, higher-order
powers of stem:[p] are sometimes used <<ref9>>. Determination of an
explicit expression is not generally possible in such a case.

NOTE: There is more than one way to express the measurement model
<<eq_12,droploc%>>. For instance, in place of the form <<eq_12,droploc%>>,
the model based on equating to zero the difference between the left-
and right-hand sides of model <<eq_11,droploc%>> could be used. The
efficiency and stability of the numerical solution of the measurement
model depends on the choice made.

NOTE: More complete models of the pressure generated by a pressure
balance can also be considered <<ref17>>, which include, for example,
a correction to account for surface tension effects.

NOTE: Not all the input quantities appear in each equation, with the
jth equation involving only
stem:[A_0, lambda, alpha, delta theta_j, m_{w, j}, rho_a, rho_w] and
stem:[g_{ell}].
====

.Reflection coefficient measured by a microwave reflectometer (approach 2)
====
Another approach to <<ex_6.2.2_2>> is to relate the input quantity
stem:[bb X -= (a_R, a_I, b_R, b_I, c_R, c_I, W_R, W_I)^{sf(T)}] and
the output quantity stem:[bb Y -= (Gamma_R, Gamma_I)^{sf(T)}] using
the bivariate measurement model

[[eq_13]]
[stem]
++++
h_1(bb Y,bb X) = 0, " " " " h_2(bb Y,bb X) = 0,
++++

where stem:[h_1(bb Y,bb X)] and stem:[h_2(bb Y,bb X)] are, respectively,
the real and imaginary parts of

[stem%unnumbered]
++++
(bb(cW) + 1) Gamma - (bb(aW) + bb(b)).
++++

An advantage of this approach is that the calculation of derivatives
and thence sensitivity coefficients is more straightforward.

An estimate stem:[y -= (hat(Gamma)_R, hat(Gamma)_I)^{sf(T)}] of the
(complex) reflection coefficient is given by setting
stem:[ bb X = bb x] in equations <<eq_13,droploc%>> and solving them
numerically.

The covariance matrix stem:[bb(U_y)] of dimension stem:[2 xx 2] associated
with stem:[bb y] is evaluated from <<eq_8>>, where stem:[bb(C_y)]
is the sensitivity matrix of dimension stem:[2 xx 2] containing the
partial derivatives stem:[del h_{ell}//del y_j],
stem:[Gamma = 1, 2], stem:[j = 1, 2], and stem:[bb(C_x)] is the sensitivity
matrix of dimension stem:[2 xx 8] containing the partial derivatives
stem:[del h_{ell}//del X_i], stem:[Gamma = 1, 2],
stem:[i = 1, ..., 8], both evaluated at stem:[bb X = bb x] and
stem:[bb Y = bb y], and stem:[bb(U_x)] is the covariance matrix of
dimension stem:[8 xx 8] associated with stem:[bb x].
====

.Reflectometer calibration
====
The calibration of a reflectometer (<<ex_6.2.2_2>>) is typically undertaken
by measuring values stem:[bb W] of the uncorrected reflection coefficient
corresponding to a number of standards with reflection coefficients
stem:[bb Gamma]. Often, three standards are used, giving the three
(complex) simultaneous equations

[[eq_14]]
[stem]
++++
(bb(c) bb(W)_j + 1) bb(Gamma)_j - (bb(a) bb(W)_j + bb(b)) = 0,
++++

for stem:[j = 1, 2, 3]. Separation of these equations into real and
imaginary parts gives rise to six simultaneous linear equations that
are solved for estimates of the real and imaginary parts of the calibration
coefficients stem:[bb(a)], stem:[bb(b)] and stem:[bb(c)] given estimates
of the real and imaginary parts of the uncorrected reflection coefficients
stem:[bb(W)_j] and of the reflection coefficients stem:[bb(Gamma)_j]
for the standards.

In terms of the general notation, stem:[N = 12], stem:[m = 6],
stem:[bb X -= (W_{1, "R"}, W_{1, "I"}, Gamma_{1, "R"}, Gamma_{1, "I"}, W_{2,"R"}, W_{2,"I"}, Gamma_{2,"R"}, Gamma_{2,"I"}, W_{3,"R"}, W_{3,"I"}, Gamma_{3,"R"}, Gamma_{3,"I"})^{sf(T)}]
and stem:[bb Y -= (a_R, a_I, b_R, b_I, c_R, c_I)^{sf(T)}].

The input and output quantities are related by a multivariate measurement
model, where, for stem:[j = 1, 2, 3], stem:[h_{2j-1}(bb Y,bb X)] and
stem:[h_{2j}(bb Y,bb X)] are, respectively, the real and imaginary
parts of the left-hand side of <<eq_14>>.

An estimate
stem:[bb y -= (hat a_R, hat a_I, hat b_R, hat b_I, hat c_R, hat c_I)^{sf(T)}]
of the (complex) calibration coefficients is given by using the estimates
of stem:[bb W_j] and stem:[bb Gamma_j] in <<eq_14>> and solving these
equations numerically.

The covariance matrix stem:[bb(U_y)] of dimension stem:[6 xx 6] associated
with stem:[bb y] is evaluated from <<eq_8>>, where stem:[bb(C_y)]
is the sensitivity matrix of dimension stem:[6 xx 6] containing the
partial derivatives stem:[del h_{ell}//del y_j], stem:[ell = 1, ..., 6],
stem:[j = 1, ..., 6], and stem:[bb(C_x)] is the sensitivity matrix
of dimension stem:[6 xx 12] containing the partial derivatives del
stem:[h_{ell}//del X_i], stem:[ell = 1, ..., 6], stem:[i = 1, ..., 12],
both evaluated at stem:[bb X = bb x] and stem:[bb Y = bb y], and stem:[bb(U_x)]
is the covariance matrix of dimension stem:[12 xx 12]  associated
with stem:[bb x].

NOTE: If a computer system capable of operating with complex quantities
is available, separation of these equations into real and imaginary
parts is unnecessary. The equations can be solved "directly" for stem:[bb a],
stem:[bb b] and stem:[bb c].

NOTE: The jth equation involves only the four input quantities
stem:[W_{j,R}, W_{j,I}, Gamma_{j,R}] and stem:[Gamma_{j,I}].
====

<<annexA>> covers the algebraically efficient determination of the
partial derivatives of first order of complex multivariate measurement
functions. These derivatives are needed in a particularization of
the law of propagation of uncertainty to such models. The treatment
can be extended to complex multivariate measurement models in general.[[uncertainty]]

.Reflection coefficient measured by a microwave reflectometer (approach 3)
====
Consider again <<ex_6.2.2_2>>.

The complex output quantity stem:[bb(Y -= Gamma)] is related to the
complex input quantity
stem:[bb X = (bb X_1, ...,bb X4)^{sf(T)} -= (bb a,bb b,bb W)^{sf(T)}]
by the measurement model <<eq_5,droploc%>>. Using <<annexA>>, stem:[bb(C_x)],
a sensitivity matrix of dimension stem:[2 xx 8], is given by evaluating

[stem%unnumbered]
++++
bb(C_X) = [bb(C_a), bb(C_b), bb(C_c), bb(C_W)],
++++

with

[stem%unnumbered]
++++
bb(C_t) = bb M ({del bb Gamma}/{del bb t}), " " " " t -= bb(a), bb(b), bb(c), bb(W),
++++

at the estimate stem:[bb x] of stem:[bb X]. For instance, since

[[eq_15]]
[stem]
++++
{del bb Gamma}/{del bb b} = 1/{bb(cW) + 1},
++++

the further use of <<annexA>> gives

[stem%unnumbered]
++++
bb(C_b) = [(Q_R, -Q_I),(Q_I, Q_R)],
++++

where stem:[Q_{"R"}] and stem:[Q_{"I"}] denote the real and imaginary
parts, respectively, of the right-hand side of <<eq_15>>.

The covariance matrix stem:[bb(U_y)] of dimension stem:[2 xx 2] associated
with stem:[bb y -= hat(bb Gamma)], where

[stem%unnumbered]
++++
bb(U_y) = [(u(hat Gamma_R, hat Gamma_R),u(hat Gamma_R,hat Gamma_I)),(u(hat Gamma_I, hat Gamma_R),u(hat Gamma_I,hat Gamma_I))] -=
[(u^2(hat Gamma_R),u(hat Gamma_R,hat Gamma_I)),(u(hat Gamma_I,hat Gamma_R),u^2(hat Gamma_I))],
++++

is evaluated from <<eq_A.1>> of <<annexA>>, where stem:[bb(U_x)] is
the covariance matrix of dimension stem:[8 xx 8] associated with
stem:[bb x].

[[sec_6.5]]
=== Coverage region for a vector output quantity
(((coverage region,from GUM uncertainty framework)))

[[sec_6.5.1]]
==== General

[[sec_6.5.1.1]]
===== {blank}

In electrical metrology, for example, it is appropriate to treat a
vector output quantity as a single entity and in dealing with summaries
of the joint probability distribution for this quantity to maintain
as much information as possible.

[[sec_6.5.1.2]]
===== {blank}

Given an estimate stem:[bb y] of the output quantity
stem:[bb Y = (Y_1, ..., Y_m)^{sf(T)}], its associated covariance matrix
stem:[bb(U_y)] and a coverage probability stem:[p], it is required
to specify a region stem:[R_{bb Y}] in _m_-dimensional space that
contains stem:[bb Y] with probability stem:[p].

[[sec_6.5.1.3]]
===== {blank}
(((output quantity,probability density function (PDF) for)))
(((principle of maximum entropy)))
(((state of knowledge)))

If stem:[bb y] and stem:[bb(U_y)] constitute the only available information
about stem:[bb Y], according to the principle of maximum entropy the
Gaussian PDF stem:[N(bb y, bb(U_y))] is used to describe the state
of knowledge of stem:[bb Y] [<<JCGM_101_2008,clause=6.4.8>>].

NOTE: This choice of PDF is consistent, within the GUM uncertainty
framework, with the characterization of a single scalar output quantity
stem:[Y] by a univariate Gaussian PDF when the associated degrees
of freedom is infinite.

[[sec_6.5.1.4]]
===== {blank}
(((coverage probability)))
(((coverage region)))
(((output quantity,probability density function (PDF) for)))

In general, once the PDF for stem:[bb Y] is established, either the
coverage probability for a specified coverage region or a coverage
region for a specified coverage probability can be determined. Doing
so is straightforward for a Gaussian PDF (see <<sec_6.5.2>>, <<sec_6.5.3>>
and <<sec_6.5.4>>). For other PDFs, a numerical method such as a Monte
Carlo method (see <<sec_7>>) is helpful in obtaining approximate solutions
that are acceptable for practical purposes.

[[sec_6.5.1.5]]
===== {blank}

The determination of a coverage region for a bivariate quantity
(see <<sec_6.5.2>>) is used to motivate the treatment for the more
general multivariate case (see <<sec_6.5.3>>). Consideration is also
given to the determination of a coverage region for a quantity representing
the average of a set of indication values regarded as random draws
made independently from a multivariate Gaussian distribution
(see <<sec_6.5.4>>).

[[sec_6.5.2]]
==== Bivariate case
(((coverage region,bivariate)))

[[sec_6.5.2.1]]
===== {blank}

The problem of specifying bivariate coverage regions illustrates the
aspects that distinguish the determination of multivariate coverage
regions from the univariate case. Consider a point
stem:[bb Y = (Y_1, Y_2)^{sf(T)}] in the plane with rectangular co-ordinates
stem:[Y_1] (abscissa) and stem:[Y_2] (ordinate). Both co-ordinates
of stem:[bb Y] are measured with the same calibrated instrument. The
information about stem:[bb Y] constitutes estimates stem:[y_1] and
stem:[y_2] of the co-ordinates, standard uncertainties stem:[u(y_1)]
and stem:[u(y_2)] associated with the estimates, as well as the covariance
stem:[u(y_1, y_2)] due to the use of the same instrument in obtaining
the estimates.

[[sec_6.5.2.2]]
===== {blank}

In the context of the GUM uncertainty framework, as there is no other
information, the joint PDF stem:[g_{Y_1, Y_2 (eta_1, eta_2)] characterizing
stem:[bb Y] is the bivariate Gaussian PDF stem:[N(bb y, bb(U_y))]
(see <<sec_6.5.1.3>>) with

[stem%unnumbered]
++++
bb y = [(y_1),(y_2)], " " " "  bb(U_y) = [(u^2(y_1), u(y_1,y_2)),(u(y_2,y_1), u^2(y_2))]
++++

[[sec_6.5.2.3]]
===== {blank}
(((chi-squared distribution)))
(((coverage interval)))
(((coverage region,elliptical)))
(((coverage region,rectangular)))
(((coverage region,smallest)))

Two particular coverage regions are considered:

. [[item_6.5.2.3_a]]An ellipse centred at stem:[bb y] <<ref19,page=38>>:
+
--

[[eq_16]]
[stem]
++++
(bb(eta - y))^{sf T} bb(U_y)^{-1}(bb(eta - y)) = k_p^2,
++++

with stem:[k_p] a constant determined such that the area under the
PDF over the elliptical region is equal to stem:[p]. With this approach,
the mutual dependence of stem:[Y_1] and stem:[Y_2] is taken into account.

When stem:[bb Y] is characterized by a (bivariate) Gaussian probability
distribution, the quantity

[[eq_17]]
[stem]
++++
(bb(Y - y))^{sf T} bb(U_y)^{-1} (bb(Y - y))
++++

is characterized by a chi-squared distribution with two degrees of
freedom. It follows that stem:[k_p^2] is given by an upper percentage
point of this chi-squared distribution, and satisfies

[stem%unnumbered]
++++
p = "Pr"(sf(X)_2^2 <= k_p^2),
++++

where stem:[sf(X)_2^2] has a chi-squared distribution with two degrees
of freedom. For the coverage probability stem:[p = 0.95],
stem:[k_p = 2.45] (see <<sec_6.5.3>>),
--

. [[item_6.5.2.3_b]]A rectangle centred at stem:[bb y] with sides
parallel to the axes and equal to the lengths of separately determined
coverage intervals for stem:[Y_1] and stem:[Y_2]. The coverage interval
for stem:[Y_1] is determined from the PDF for stem:[Y_1] given by
marginalization.
+
--
[[eq_18]]
[stem]
++++
g_{Y_1}(eta_1) = int_{-oo}^{oo} g_{Y_1}, Y_2 (eta_1, eta_2) "d"eta_2,
++++

irrespective of the state of knowledge of stem:[Y_2], and similarly
for the coverage interval for stem:[Y_2]. The coverage intervals
stem:[y_j +- k_q u(y_j)] are determined for coverage probability

[[eq_19]]
[stem]
++++
q = 1 - (1 - p)//2 = (1 + p)//2 .
++++

This rectangle constitutes a coverage region for stem:[bb Y] corresponding
to a coverage probability of at least stem:[p] <<ref5>>.

When stem:[bb Y] is characterized by a (bivariate) Gaussian probability
distribution, the marginal distribution <<eq_18,droploc%>> for stem:[Y_1]
is Gaussian as is that for stem:[Y_2]. It follows that stem:[k_q]
is given by an upper percentage point of the standard Gaussian distribution,
and satisfies

[stem%unnumbered]
++++
q = "Pr"(|Z| <= k_q),
++++

where stem:[Z] has the standard Gaussian distribution stem:[N(0, 1)].
For the coverage probability stem:[p = 0.95], the coverage intervals
are stem:[y_j +- k_q u(y_j)], for stem:[j = 1, 2], with
stem:[q = 0.975] and stem:[k_q = 2.24] (see <<sec_6.5.3>>).
--

NOTE: In the context of the GUM uncertainty framework, the elliptical
coverage region given in <<item_6.5.2.3_a>> is the smallest
stem:[100p %] coverage region.

NOTE: When stem:[Y_1] and stem:[Y_2] are mutually independent, stem:[q]
in <<eq_19>> can be replaced by stem:[q = p^{1//2}].

[[ex_6.5.2.3_1]]
====
Consider a bivariate quantity stem:[bb Y] characterized by the Gaussian
probability distribution stem:[N(bb y,bb(U_y))], where

[stem%unnumbered]
++++
bb y = [(0),(0)], " " " " bb(U_y) = [(2.0,0.0),(0.0,1.0)]
++++

<<figure3>> (left) shows 95 % elliptical and rectangular coverage
regions for stem:[bb Y], obtained as in <<item_6.5.2.3_a>> and
<<item_6.5.2.3_b>>. Also shown are 1 000 points representing random
draws from this probability distribution. The ellipse is the smallest
coverage region for the stipulated coverage probability. 950 of the
1 000 points are contained within the elliptical coverage region,
which has area stem:[26.6 " unit"^2], and 953 within the rectangular
region, which has area stem:[28.4" unit"^2].
====

[[ex_6.5.2.3_2]]
====
Consider a bivariate quantity stem:[bb Y] characterized by the Gaussian
probability distribution stem:[N(bb y,bb(U_y))], where

[stem%unnumbered]
++++
bb y = [(0),(0)], " " " " bb(U_y) = [(2.0,1.9),(1.9,2.0)]
++++

<<figure3>> (right) shows 95 % elliptical and rectangular coverage
regions for stem:[bb Y], obtained as in <<item_6.5.2.3_a>> and
<<item_6.5.2.3_b>>. Unlike example 1, the component quantities stem:[Y_1]
and stem:[Y_2] of stem:[bb Y] are (positively) correlated. 957 of
the 1 000 points are contained within the elliptical coverage region,
which has area stem:[11.8 " unit"^2], and 972 within the rectangular
region, which has area stem:[40.1 " unit"^2], indicating that the
coverage probability for the rectangular region exceeds 0.95. The
rectangular region, whose shape does not reflect the correlated component
quantities and the distribution of the randomly drawn points, might
be considered inappropriate as a coverage region for stem:[bb Y].
A rectangle with sides parallel to the axes of the ellipse would have
smaller area and might be considered more appropriate, but might be
inconvenient since it would be expressed in terms of variables that
would be artificial in terms of the application.
====

For more than two quantities, coverage regions are no longer easily
visualized, but can be constructed in an analogous manner to those
described in <<item_6.5.2.3_a>> and <<item_6.5.2.3_b>> for the bivariate
case. A region stem:[R_{bb Y}] in the _m_-dimensional space of the
output quantity stem:[Y = (Y_1, ..., Y_m)^{sf(T)}] is required such
that the probability that stem:[bb Y] lies in stem:[R_{bb Y}] is equal
to the stipulated coverage probability stem:[p]. The following two
forms of stem:[R_{bb Y}], analogous to those described in <<item_6.5.2.3_a>>
and <<item_6.5.2.3_b>>, can be considered.

a chi-squared distribution with m degrees of freedom. It follows that

stem:[q = 1 - (1 - p)//m]. This hyper-rectangle constitutes a coverage

is calculated following marginalization.
+

distribution, the marginal distribution for each stem:[Y_j] is Gaussian.
It follows that the coverage factor stem:[k_q] is given by an upper

++++

giving

[stem%unnumbered]

stem:[bb mu] and covariance matrix stem:[bb sum]. Define the average
and covariance matrix

[stem%unnumbered]
++++
bb A = 1/n (bb Y_1 + cdots + bb Y_n), " " " " V = 1/n [(bb Y_1 - bb A)(bb Y_1 - bb A)^{sf(T)} + cdots + (bb Y_n - bb A)(bb Y_n - bb A)^{sf(T)}],
++++

of dimensions stem:[m xx 1] and stem:[m xx m], respectively. Then,
the random variable

[stem%unnumbered]
++++
{n - m}/m (bb A - bb mu)^{sf(T)} bb(V)^{-1}(bb A - bb mu)
++++

has an stem:[F_{m,n-m}] distribution <<ref19,locality:corollary=3.5.2.1>>,
the so-called Fisher-Snedecor distribution with stem:[m] and
stem:[n - m] degrees of freedom.

NOTE: The counterpart of this result for a univariate quantity is
as follows: for independent random variables stem:[Y_1, ..., Y_n],
each characterized by a univariate Gaussian probability distribution
with expectation stem:[mu] and variance stem:[sigma^2],
stem:[(n - 1)^{1//2}(A - mu)//S] has a _t_-distribution with
stem:[n - 1] degrees of freedom, where now

[stem%unnumbered]
++++
A = 1/n (Y_1 + cdots + Y_n), " " " " S^2 = 1/n [(Y_1 - A)^2 + cdots + (Y_n - A)^2].
++++
