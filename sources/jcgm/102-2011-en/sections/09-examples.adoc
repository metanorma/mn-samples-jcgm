=== Illustrations of aspects of this Supplement

[[sec_9.1.1]]
==== {blank}
(((analytical solution)))
(((measurement model,linear)))

The first example (see <<sec_9.2>>) is a linear measurement model
in which the output quantities of the measurement model depend on
a common effect and on effects that are different for the output quantities.
This is an example for which analytical solutions are available for
some special cases.

[[sec_9.1.2]]
==== {blank}
(((analytical solution)))
(((measurement model,non-linear)))
(((quantity,complex)))

The second example (see <<sec_9.3>>) is a non-linear measurement model
concerning the transformation from a Cartesian representation (in
terms of real and imaginary parts) of a complex quantity to a polar
representation (in terms of magnitude and phase) of the quantity.
This is an example for which analytical solutions are available for
some special cases <<ref6>>.

[[sec_9.1.3]]
==== {blank}
(((indication values)))
(((measurement model,non-linear)))

The third example (see <<sec_9.4>>), constituting a further non-linear
measurement model, is that in the GUM concerned with the simultaneous
measurement of resistance and reactance [<<JCGM_100_2008,clause=H.2>>].
The example illustrates the treatment of a series of simultaneous
indication values of a vector quantity that have been obtained independently.

[[sec_9.1.4]]
==== {blank}
(((measurement model,multivariate)))
(((measurement model,univariate)))

The fourth example (see <<sec_9.5>>) is concerned with the measurement
of Celsius temperature using a resistance thermometer. The example
illustrates the treatment of a univariate measurement model and a
multivariate measurement model.

[[sec_9.1.5]]
==== {blank}

Many of the figures used to illustrate the results for the examples
are best viewed in colour. In the figures showing contours, contours
with a common colour correspond to the same contour value. The correspondence
between contours of the same colour for contour plots within the same
figure is applied throughout this Supplement unless a statement is
made to the contrary. When two or more figures are used to compare
results, such as those from the GUM uncertainty framework and a Monte
Carlo method, the same axes are generally used. There are some exceptions
when the PDFs, etc. for the methods are very different.

[[sec_9.1.6]]
==== {blank}
(((contour smoothing)))
(((Monte Carlo method (MCM),primary output from)))

Since the primary output from MCM is the set of stem:[M] values of
the vector quantity stem:[bb Y] gathered in the matrix G of dimension
stem:[m xx M] (see <<sec_7.1.6>>), it is often desirable to summarize
them by an approximation to the corresponding probability density
function, and to depict this function by its contour lines. The figures
in this clause show such contour lines in cases where the output quantity
is of dimension stem:[m = 2]. The contour lines should be drawn in
ways that guarantee that, as the number M of draws from the probability
distribution of the output quantity grows large, they converge to
the contours of the corresponding probability density function: doing
so requires appropriate smoothing <<ref22>>, <<ref24>>. Some contour
diagrams shown in this clause are computed directly from an approximation
to the corresponding probability density function. For others an appropriate
smoothing algorithm is applied and the resulting smoothed contours
drawn. In one instance (<<figure10>>) unsmoothed and smoothed contours
are shown to indicate the effect of the smoothing algorithm.

[[sec_9.2]]
=== Additive measurement model

[[sec_9.2.1]]
==== Formulation

This example (compare <<ex_7.7.2>>) considers the additive (linear)
bivariate measurement model

[[eq_24]]
[stem]
++++
Y_1 = X_1 + X_3," " " " Y_2 = X_2 + X_3
++++

for three different sets of PDFs stem:[g_{X_i}(xi_i)] assigned to
the input quantities stem:[X_i], regarded as independent. The measurement
model has three input quantities stem:[X_1], stem:[X_2] and stem:[X_3],
with stem:[X_3] representing an effect that is common to the output
quantities stem:[Y_1] and stem:[Y_2], and stem:[X_1] and stem:[X_2]
effects that are different for stem:[Y_1] and stem:[Y_2]. For the
first set of input PDFs (see <<sec_9.2.2>>), each stem:[g_{X_i}(xi_i)]
is chosen to be a standard Gaussian distribution with stem:[X_i] having
expectation zero and standard deviation unity. The second set of PDFs
(see <<sec_9.2.3>>) is identical to the first, but stem:[g_{X_3}(xi_3)]
is a rectangular distribution, also with stem:[X_3] having expectation
zero and standard deviation unity. The third set of PDFs (see <<sec_9.2.4>>)
is identical to the second except that stem:[X_3] has standard deviation
three, representing a dominant common effect.

The (generalized) GUM uncertainty framework gives the estimate
stem:[bb y = (0, 0)^{sf(T)}] of stem:[bb Y]. The covariance matrix
associated with this estimate is

[stem%unnumbered]
++++
bb(U_y) = [(2,1),(1,2)],
++++

obtained by applying <<eq_3>>, namely stem:[bb(U_y) = bb(C_x) bb(U_x)C_x^{sf(T)}], with

[stem%unnumbered]

++++

and stem:[y_2] (see <<sec_3.21>>) is stem:[r(y_1, y_2) = 0.5]. 95%
coverage regions for stem:[bb Y] taking the forms of an ellipse and
a rectangle are defined, respectively, by coverage factors
stem:[k_p = 2.45] (<<table1>>) and stem:[k_q = 2.24] (<<table2>>).
These results are summarized in row 1 (GUF) of <<table3>>. In the
context of the GUM uncertainty framework, given the information available,
stem:[bb Y] is characterized by the bivariate Gaussian PDF stem:[N(bb(y),bb(U_y))].

[[sec_9.2.2.3]]
===== {blank}

The application of MCM with, respectively, stem:[M = 10^5, 10^6] and
stem:[10^7] trials gives the results summarized in rows 2 to 4 (MCM)
of <<table3>>. The fourth and fifth numerical values of stem:[M]
(stem:[0.35 xx 10^6] and stem:[0.45 xx 10^6]) given in rows 5 and

(see <<note_8.3_3>>). For the applications of the adaptive procedure,
it is required that all the results, i.e.
stem:[y_1, y_2, u(y_1), u(y_2), r(y_1, y_2), k_p] and stem:[k_q],
have stabilized in a statistical sense.

[[sec_9.2.2.5]]

<<figure8>> (left) shows contours of the bivariate Gaussian PDF for
stem:[bb Y] obtained from the GUM uncertainty framework (or obtained
analytically). The contours take the form of ellipses with equations

[stem%unnumbered]

from this description of the approximate PDF for the same values of

in <<figure8>> (right) are not smoothed (see <<sec_9.1.6>>).

NOTE: <<figure8>> and subsequent contour plots include a colour bar

[[sec_9.2.2.7]]

(((contour smoothing)))

<<figure10>> (left) shows the contours of the approximation to the

needed to ensure that the contours in a graphical representation of
the approximate PDF for stem:[bb Y] obtained using MCM are smooth.
A similar effect arises for a single scalar output quantity, where
a large value for M and many classes (or bins), are generally needed
to ensure that the approximate PDF displayed as a histogram (or scaled
frequency distribution) is smooth.

[[sec_9.2.2.8]]
===== {blank}

The validation procedure of <<sec_8>> is applied to validate the results
obtained from the GUM uncertainty framework against those from the
two applications of the adaptive Monte Carlo calculation. The procedure
is applied with stem:[n_{"dig"}], the number of significant decimal
digits regarded as meaningful in the numerical value of a result,
set equal to two. In this case, the numerical tolerances associated
with the results are, respectively,

[stem%unnumbered]
++++
delta_1 = delta_2 = 0.05, " " rho = 0.05, " " kappa_p = kappa_q = 0.05.
++++

The results obtained from the GUM uncertainty framework were validated
in both instances. Excepting stem:[k_q], agreement between the results
would be expected because all the conditions hold for the application
of the GUM uncertainty framework. The evaluation of stem:[k_q] in
the GUM uncertainty framework does not take account of the mutual
dependence between stem:[Y_1] and stem:[Y_2] (see <<sec_6.5.2.3>>).
Nevertheless, the values for stem:[k_q] obtained agree to the required
number of significant decimal digits.

[[sec_9.3]]
=== Co-ordinate system transformation

[[sec_9.3.1]]
==== Formulation

[[sec_9.3.1.1]]
===== {blank}

Consider a complex quantity Z represented in Cartesian form

[stem%unnumbered]
++++
X_1 + "i"X_2, 
++++

where stem:[X_1 -= Z_{"R"}] and stem:[X_2 -= Z_{"I"}] are, respectively,
the real and imaginary parts of stem:[Z], or in polar form

[stem%unnumbered]
++++
R(cos theta + "i"sin theta) = R e^{"i"theta},
++++

[[figure13]]
.Counterpart of <<figure11>> for an input quantity stem:[X_3] with a standard deviation of three (<<sec_9.2.4>>)
image::figure13.png[]

[[figure14]]
.Counterpart of <<figure12>> for an input quantity stem:[X_3] with a standard deviation of three (<<sec_9.2.4>>)
image::figure14.png[]

where stem:[R] and stem:[theta] are, respectively, the magnitude and
phase of stem:[bb Z]. This example considers the transformation from
Cartesian to polar representations of stem:[bb Z] described by the
bivariate measurement model

[[eq_25]]
[stem]
++++
Y_1^2 = X_1^2 + X_2^2, " " " " tan Y_2 = X_2//X_1,
++++

with input quantity stem:[X = (X_1, X_2)^{sf(T)} -= (Z_{"R"}, Z_{"I"})^{sf(T)}]
and output quantity stem:[bb Y = (Y_1, Y_2)^{sf(T)} -= (R, theta)^{sf(T)}].

NOTE: <<eq_25>> constitute a bivariate measurement model for stem:[R]
and stem:[theta]. Since stem:[R] is a magnitude, and is known to be
non-negative, it is determined uniquely by the positive square root
of stem:[R^2]. By using a "four quadrant inverse tangent function"
(often denoted by "atan2") stem:[theta] is determined uniquely to
satisfy stem:[-pi < theta <= pi]. Thus <<eq_25>> can be cast as a
bivariate measurement model.

[[sec_9.3.1.2]]
===== {blank}
(((input quantity,assigning a probability density function (PDF) to)))

Given are estimates stem:[x_1] and stem:[x_2] of the quantities stem:[X_1]
and stem:[X_2], obtained from a suitable measuring system, and associated
standard uncertainties stem:[u(x_1)] and stem:[u(x_2)] and covariance
stem:[u(x_1, x_2) = ru(x_1)u(x_2)], where stem:[r = r(x_1, x_2)] denotes
the associated correlation coefficient [<<JCGM_100_2008,clause=5.2.2>>].
On the basis of this information [<<JCGM_101_2008,clause=6.4.8.1>>],
stem:[X] is assigned a bivariate Gaussian PDF, with expectation and
covariance matrix

[stem%unnumbered]
++++
[(x_1),(x_2)], " " [(u^2(x_1),ru(x_1)u(x_2)),(ru(x_1)u(x_2),u^2(x_2))].
++++

The quantities stem:[X_1] and stem:[X_2] are assumed to have dimension
one.

[[sec_9.3.1.3]]
===== {blank}

The determination of an estimate stem:[bb y] of stem:[bb Y] and the
associated covariance matrix stem:[bb(U_y)] is considered for different
choices of stem:[x_1, x_2, u(x_1), u(x_2)] and stem:[r(x_1, x_2)].

[[sec_9.3.1.4]]
===== {blank}

Six cases are considered, in all of which stem:[x_2] is taken as zero
and stem:[u(x_1) = u(x_2) = u_x = 0.010]. The first three of these
cases correspond to taking stem:[x_1 = 0.001, 0.010] and 0.100, each
with stem:[r(x_1, x_2) = 0] (see <<sec_9.3.2>>). The second three
cases correspond to taking the same stem:[x_1], but with
stem:[r(x_1, x_2) = 0.9] (see <<sec_9.3.3>>). <<figure15>> shows the
contours of the joint PDFs assigned to the input quantities stem:[bb X]
for case 1 (stem:[x_1 = 0.001] and stem:[r(x_1, x_2) = 0]) and case
4 (stem:[x_1 = 0.001] and stem:[r(x_1, x_2) = 0.9]). (In this figure
a common set of contour values is not used for the two contour plots.)
The contours of the joint PDFs for the other cases are obtained by
translating the contours shown along the stem:[X_1]-axis so that they
are centred on stem:[x_1 = 0.010] (cases 2 and 5) or stem:[x_1 = 0.100]
(cases 3 and 6).

[[sec_9.3.2]]
==== Propagation and summarizing: zero covariance

[[sec_9.3.2.1]]
===== General

[[sec_9.3.2.1.1]]
====== {blank}

The evaluation of uncertainty is treated by applying the propagation
of distributions (a) analytically (for purposes of comparison), (b)
using the (generalized) GUM uncertainty framework, and (c) using MCM.

[[sec_9.3.2.1.2]]
====== {blank}

The (joint) PDF for stem:[bb Y], and the marginal PDFs for stem:[Y_1]
and stem:[Y_2], can be obtained analytically in the case that stem:[X_1]
is described by the Gaussian distribution stem:[N(x_1, u_x^2)] and
stem:[X_2] by stem:[N(x_2, u_x^2)], with stem:[X_1] and stem:[X_2]
mutually independent. See <<sec_C.2>>.

[[sec_9.3.2.1.3]]

[[sec_9.4]]
=== Simultaneous measurement of resistance and reactance

[[sec_9.4.1]]
==== Formulation

[[sec_9.4.1.1]]
===== {blank}

The resistance stem:[R] and reactance stem:[X] of a circuit element
are determined by measuring the amplitude stem:[V] of a sinusoidally-alternating
potential difference across its terminals, the amplitude stem:[I]
of the alternating current passing through it, and the phase angle
phi of the alternating potential difference relative to the alternating
current. The input quantities are stem:[V], stem:[I] and stem:[phi]
and the output quantities are the three impedance components stem:[R],
stem:[X] and stem:[Z], where stem:[Z^2 = R^2 + X^2].

[[sec_9.4.1.2]]
===== {blank}

The application of Ohm's law gives the trivariate measurement model

[[eq_26]]
[stem]
++++
R = V/I cos phi, " " " " X = V/I sin phi, " " " " Z = V/I.
++++

as the relationship between the input quantity
stem:[bb X = (X_1, X_2, X_3)^{sf(T)} -= (V, I, phi)^{sf(T)}] and the
output quantity stem:[bb Y = (Y_1, Y_2, Y_3)^{sf(T)} -= (R,X,Z)^{sf(T)}].

NOTE: For reasons of simplicity, any systematic errors that may be
present in stem:[V], stem:[I] and stem:[phi] are ignored.

NOTE: In the GUM, reactance is denoted by stem:[X], the notation also
used here. The reactance stem:[X], a component of the vector output
quantity stem:[bb Y], is not to be confused with stem:[bb X], the
vector input quantity.

[[sec_9.4.1.3]]
===== {blank}

A series of stem:[n = 6] sets stem:[x_1, ..., x_n] of simultaneous
indication values of the input quantities are obtained independently
under similar conditions resulting in the data given in <<table8>>.

NOTE: In the GUM, the data consists of a series of five sets of simultaneous
indication values obtained independently (sets 1 to 5 of <<table8>>).
At least six sets are required for the covariance matrix in <<sec_9.4.2.5>>
to be defined. Set 6 in <<table8>> is therefore included, and is chosen
to be the average of sets 1 to 5. This particular choice for set 6
is not essential, but has the consequence that its inclusion does
not change the average of the series.

==== Propagation and summarizing

[[sec_9.4.2.1]]
===== {blank}

The determination of an estimate stem:[bb y] of stem:[bb Y] and the
associated covariance matrix stem:[bb(U_y)] is considered. The approach
here treats the problem defined by the measurement model <<eq_26,droploc%>>
and information about the input quantities in that model in the form
of the sets stem:[x_1, ..., x_n] of indication values obtained independently
given in <<table8>>. The GUM addresses this problem using the GUM
uncertainty framework [<<JCGM_100_2008,clause=H.2.3>>]. The treatment
here is extended to include MCM and an approach involving a multivariate
_t_-distribution (see <<sec_5.3.2>>).

[[sec_9.4.2.2]]
===== {blank}

For the purpose of applying the (generalized) GUM uncertainty framework,
estimates stem:[bb x -= (hat(V), hat(I), hat(phi))^{sf(T)}] of the
input quantities stem:[X -= (V, I, phi)^{sf(T)}] are determined as
the averages of the indication values given in <<table8>>
[<<JCGM_100_2008,clause=4.2>>].

[stem%unnumbered]
++++
bb x = 1/n (bb x_1 + cdots + bb x_n).
++++

The covariance matrix stem:[bb(U_x)] associated with stem:[bb x] contains
the variances associated with the averages [<<JCGM_100_2008,clause=4.2>>]
and the covariances associated with each pair of averages
[<<JCGM_100_2008,clause=5.2.3>>], and is evaluated from

[stem%unnumbered]
++++
bb(U_x) = 1/{n(n - 1)} bb(M), " " " " bb(M) = (bb x_1 - bb x)(bb x_1 - bb x)^{sf(T)} + cdots + (bb x_n - bb x)(bb x_n - bb x)^{sf(T)}.
++++

stem:[bb M] is the matrix of sums of squares and products. The estimates
stem:[bb x] and the associated standard uncertainties determined in
this way are given in <<table9>>, and the correlation coefficients
associated with pairs of the estimates in <<table10>>.

NOTE: In the GUM, an approach 2 is presented for this example, based
on GUM 4.1.4 note. This approach is not followed here, for the reason
given in <<sec_4.1>>.

[[sec_9.5]]
=== Measurement of Celsius temperature using a resistance thermometer

[[sec_9.5.1]]
==== General

The example described in this subclause is concerned with the measurement
of Celsius temperature by comparing the resistance of an industrial
platinum resistance thermometer with that of a standard resistor using
a resistance bridge. The measurement of a single Celsius temperature
is described by a univariate measurement model (see <<sec_9.5.2>>),
and that of several temperatures by a multivariate measurement model
(see <<sec_9.5.3>>). The example illustrates the treatment of these
(univariate and multivariate) measurement models using the (generalized)
GUM uncertainty framework.

[[sec_9.5.2]]
==== Measurement of a single Celsius temperature

[[sec_9.5.2.1]]
===== {blank}
(((calibration)))

The Celsius temperature stem:[theta] is measured by comparing the
resistance stem:[R(theta)] of a resistance thermometer with the resistance
stem:[R_S] of a standard resistor using a resistance bridge. In the
temperature interval from 0 °C to 30 °C, the resistance of the thermometer
is approximated by a parabolic function of its Celsius temperature
stem:[theta].

[[eq_29]]
[stem]
++++
R(theta) = (1 + A theta + B theta^2)R_0,
++++

where stem:[R_0], stem:[A] and stem:[B] are determined from a calibration
of the thermometer. Estimates of stem:[R_0], stem:[A] and stem:[B]
and the associated standard uncertainties are given in <<table12>>,

[[sec_9.5.2.3]]
===== {blank}

The quantity measured with the resistance bridge is the resistance
ratio

[[eq_30]]
[stem]
++++
r = {R(theta)}/{R_{"S"}} .
++++

The measured value of stem:[r] and the associated standard uncertainty
are given in <<table12>>. The resistance ratio is independent of the
parameters stem:[R_0], stem:[A] and stem:[B] of the resistance thermometer
and the resistance stem:[R_{"S"}] of the standard resistor; there
are thus no further non-zero correlation coefficients beyond those
given in <<table13>>.

[[sec_9.5.2.4]]
===== {blank}

By combining <<eq_29;and!eq_30>>, the following measurement model
for the Celsius temperature stem:[theta] is obtained.

[[eq_31]]
[stem]
++++
(1 + A theta + B theta^2) R_0 - r R_{"S"} = 0 .
++++

In terms of the general notation, stem:[N = 5], stem:[m = 1],
stem:[bb X -= (R_0, A, B, R_{"S"}, r)^{sf(T)}], stem:[Y -= theta],
and

[stem%unnumbered]
++++
h(Y, bb(X)) = (1 + A theta + B theta^2)R_0 - r R_{"S"}.
++++

NOTE: By considering expressions for the roots of a quadratic equation,
transformation of the measurement model <<eq_31,droploc%>> to explicit
form is possible. However, the numerical evaluation of the transformed
model can suffer from subtractive cancellation, and the evaluation
of sensitivity coefficients is made more difficult by the transformation.

[[sec_9.5.2.5]]
===== {blank}

The estimate stem:[y -= hat(theta)] of the Celsius temperature generating
the measured resistance ratio stem:[r] is found by inserting the estimates
given in <<table12>> into <<eq_31>> and solving this equation. The
solution obtained is stem:[hat(theta) = 20.023 2 " °C"].

[[sec_9.5.2.6]]
===== {blank}

The standard uncertainty stem:[u_y -= u(hat(theta))] associated with
the estimate stem:[y] is evaluated using <<eq_8>> in <<sec_6.3.1.3>>,
namely stem:[bb(C_y) bb(U_y) bb(C_y)^{sf(T)} = bb(C_x) bb(U_x) bb(C_x)^{sf(T)}].
Evaluating the matrices stem:[bb(C_y)] and stem:[bb(C_x)] in <<sec_6.3.1.3>>,
namely

[stem%unnumbered]
++++
bb(C_Y) = {del h}/{del Y} -= {del h}/{del theta} = (A + 2B theta) R_0,
++++

[stem%unnumbered]
++++
bb(C_X) = {del h}/{del bb(X)} -= [{del h}/{del R_0}, {del h}/{del A}, {del h}/{del B},{del h}/{del R_{"S"}},{del h}/{del r}] = [1 + A theta + B theta^2, R_0 theta, R_0 theta^2, -r, -R_{"S"}],
++++

at the estimates of the input quantities given in <<table12>> and
the corresponding estimate of the output quantity, gives the sensitivity
matrices

[stem%unnumbered]
++++
c_y = 0.389 Omega "°C"^{-1}
++++

and

[stem%unnumbered]
++++
bb(C_x) = [1.08, " "2.00 xx 10^3 Omega "°C", " " 4.01 Omega "°C"^2, -1.08, -1.00 xx 10^2 Omega ] .
++++

The elements of the covariance matrix stem:[bb(U_x)] associated with
the estimates of the input quantities are calculated from the standard
uncertainties in <<table12>> and the correlation coefficients in <<table13>>.
The result obtained is stem:[u(hat(theta)) = 0.004 5 " °C"].

[[sec_9.5.3.6]]
===== {blank}

The results given in <<table15>> and <<figure22>> show how the standard
uncertainty stem:[u(hat(theta)_j)] varies with the estimate stem:[hat(theta)_j]
of Celsius temperature stem:[theta_j]. The uncertainty is smallest
around 0 °C and increases rapidly for temperatures greater than 25
°C. This effect is due to the fact that the resistance thermometer
was calibrated at the temperatures 0 °C, 15 °C, 20 °C and 25 °C, and
that the Celsius temperature 0 °C during calibration was generated
using an ice bath with a standard uncertainty three times smaller
than those associated with the other three temperatures, which were
generated using an oil bath.

NOTE: In <<figure22;and!figure23>> the straight-line segments joining
the plotted points are included for purpose of visualization.
