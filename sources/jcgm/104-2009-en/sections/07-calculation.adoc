
[[cls_7]]
== The calculation (propagation and summarizing) stage of uncertainty evaluation

=== General

==== {blank}

The propagation stage of uncertainty evaluation is known as the _propagation of distributions_(((propagation,of distributions))) <<JCGM1012008,clause=5.2>>, various approaches for which are available, including

. [[item_7-1-1a]]the ((GUM uncertainty framework)), constituting the application of the ((law of propagation of uncertainty)), and the characterization of the output quantity(((quantity,output))) stem:[ii(Y)] by a Gaussian or a stem:[t]-distribution(((probability distribution,stem:[t])))(((probability distribution,Gaussian))) (see <<scls_7-2>>),

. [[item_7-1-1b]]analytic methods(((analytic method))), in which mathematical analysis is used to derive an algebraic form for the ((probability distribution))(((probability distribution,distribution function))) for stem:[ii(Y)] (see <<scls_7-3>>), and

. [[item_7-1-1c]]a ((Monte Carlo method)) (MCM), in which an approximation to the distribution function for stem:[ii(Y)] is established numerically by making random draws from the ((probability distribution))s for the input quantities(((quantity,input))), and evaluating the model(((measurement model,evaluation))) at the resulting values (see <<scls_7-4>>).


==== {blank}

For any particular uncertainty evaluation problem(((uncertainty evaluation,problem))), approach <<item_7-1-1a>>, <<item_7-1-1b>> or <<item_7-1-1c>> (or some other approach) is used, <<item_7-1-1a>> being generally approximate, <<item_7-1-1b>> exact, and <<item_7-1-1c>> providing a solution with a numerical accuracy that can be controlled.


==== {blank}

The application of approaches <<item_7-1-1a>> and <<item_7-1-1c>> to measurement functions with any number of output quantities, and general measurement models(((measurement model,general form))), is considered in <<scls_7-5>>.


[[scls_7-2]]
=== The ((GUM uncertainty framework))

[[scls_7-2-1]]
==== {blank}

The ((GUM uncertainty framework)) <<JCGM1002008,clause=(GUM) 3.4.8, 5.1>> (depicted in <<fig7>>) uses

. the best estimates(((estimate,best))) stem:[x_i] of the input quantities stem:[ii(X)_i],
. the standard uncertainties stem:[u(x_i)] associated with the stem:[x_i], and
. the ((sensitivity coefficient))s stem:[c_i] (see <<scls_4-14>>)

to form an ((estimate)) stem:[y] of the output quantity(((quantity,output))) stem:[ii(Y)] and the associated ((standard uncertainty)) stem:[u(y)].


==== {blank}

A variant <<JCGM1002008,clause=(GUM) 5.2>> of <<scls_7-2-1>> applies when the input quantities are mutually dependent(((quantity,dependent))) (not indicated in <<fig7>>). By regarding the ((probability distribution)) for stem:[ii(Y)] as Gaussian(((probability distribution,Gaussian))), a ((coverage interval)) for stem:[ii(Y)] corresponding to a specified ((coverage probability)) is also determined <<JCGM1002008,clause=(GUM) G.2>>. When the ((degrees of freedom)) <<ISO_3534-1,clause=2.54>> relating to any stem:[u(x_i)] is finite, an (effective) degrees of freedom(((degrees of freedom,effective))) relating to stem:[u(y)] is determined, and the ((probability distribution)) for stem:[ii(Y)] taken as a stem:[t]-distribution(((probability distribution,stem[t]))).


==== {blank}

There are many circumstances where the ((GUM uncertainty framework)) <<JCGM1002008,clause=(GUM) 5>> can be applied and leads to valid statements of uncertainty. If the measurement function is linear(((measurement function,linear))) in the input quantities(((quantity,input))) and the ((probability distribution))s for these quantities are Gaussian(((probability distribution,Gaussian))), the GUM uncertainty framework provides exact results <<JCGM1012008,clause=5.7>>. Even when these conditions(((GUM uncertainty framework,conditions on use))) do not hold, the approach can often work sufficiently well for practical purposes <<JCGM1012008,clause=5.8>>.


[[scls_7-2-4]]
==== {blank}

There are situations where the ((GUM uncertainty framework)) might not be satisfactory, including those where

. the measurement function is non-linear(((measurement function,non-linear))),
. the ((probability distribution))s for the input quantities are asymmetric,
. the uncertainty contributions stem:[|c_1| u(x_1),..., |c_{ii(N)}| u(x_{ii(N)})] (see <<scls_4-14>>) are not of approximately the same magnitude <<JCGM1002008,clause=(GUM) G.2.2>>, and
. the ((probability distribution)) for the output quantity(((quantity,output))) is either asymmetric(((probability distribution,asymmetric))), or not a Gaussian or a stem:[t]-distribution.

Sometimes it is hard to establish in advance that the circumstances hold for the ((GUM uncertainty framework))(((GUM uncertainty framework,conditions on use))) to apply.

==== {blank}

The use of the ((GUM uncertainty framework)) becomes more difficult when forming ((partial derivative))s (or numerical approximations to them) for a measurement model that is complicated(((measurement model,complicated))), as needed by the law of propagation of uncertainty (possibly with higher-order terms)(((law of propagation of uncertainty,higher-order terms))) <<JCGM1002008,clause=(GUM) 5>>. A valid and sometimes more readily applicable treatment is obtained by applying a suitable Monte Carlo implementation(((Monte Carlo method))) of the propagation of distributions(((propagation,of distributions))) (see <<scls_7-4>>).


[[scls_7-3]]
=== Analytic methods(((analytic method)))

[[scls_7-3-1]]
==== {blank}

Analytic methods by which an algebraic form for the ((probability distribution)) for the output quantity can be obtained do not introduce any approximation, but can be applied only in relatively simple cases. A treatment of such methods is available <<Casella2001>>, <<Dietrich1991>>. Some cases that can be so handled for a general number stem:[ii(N)] of input quantities(((quantity,input))) are linear measurement functions(((measurement function,linear))) (expression <<eq3,(3)>>), where the ((probability distribution))s for all input quantities are Gaussian(((probability distribution,Gaussian))), or all are rectangular with the same width. An instance with two input quantities(((quantity,input))) (stem:[ii(N) = 2]), for which the ((probability distribution))s for the input quantities are rectangular(((probability distribution,rectangular))), and the ((probability distribution)) for the output quantity(((quantity,output))) is trapezoidal(((probability distribution,trapezoidal))) <<Cox-Harris2006>> is illustrated in <<fig3>>.


[[scls_7-3-2]]
==== {blank}

Cases where there is one input quantity (stem:[ii(N) = 1]) can often be treated analytically, using a formula <<Rice1995>>, pages 57-61, to derive algebraically a ((probability distribution)) for the output quantity. Such cases arise in the transformation of measurement units((("measurement units, transformation"))), for example from linear to logarithmic units <<Cox-Harris2006>>, pages 95-98.


==== {blank}

An advantage of an algebraic solution is that it provides insight through displaying the dependence of the ((probability distribution)) for the output quantity(((quantity,output))) on parameters of the ((probability distribution))s for the input quantities.


[[scls_7-4]]
=== ((Monte Carlo method))

==== {blank}

<<JCGM1012008>> provides detailed information on MCM as an implementation of the propagation of distributions(((propagation,of distributions))) <<JCGM1012008,clause=5.9>>. MCM has fewer conditions associated with its use than the ((GUM uncertainty framework)) <<JCGM1012008,clause=5.10>>. <<fig8>> illustrates the procedure. <<JCGM1012008>> gives examples to compare MCM with the use of the ((GUM uncertainty framework))(((GUM uncertainty framework,compared with Monte Carlo method)))(((Monte Carlo method,comparison with the GUM uncertainty framework)))(((Monte Carlo method,conditions on use)))(((Monte Carlo method,examples))) <<JCGM1012008,clause=9>>.


==== {blank}

<<JCGM1012008>> provides an adaptive MCM(((Monte Carlo method,adaptive))) procedure, in which the number of Monte Carlo trials(((Monte Carlo method,number of trials))) is determined automatically by utilizing a measure of convergence of the overall process <<JCGM1012008,clause=7.9>>.


==== {blank}

In <<JCGM1012008>> there is a procedure that uses MCM to decide whether the application of the ((GUM uncertainty framework))(((GUM uncertainty framework,validition using MCM))) in any particular case is valid <<JCGM1012008,clause=8>>.

[[scls_7-5]]
=== Measurement models with any number of output quantities(((quantity,output)))(((measurement model,with any number of output quantities)))

==== {blank}

In order to evaluate the uncertainties and ((covariance))s associated with ((estimate))s of the output quantities(((quantity,output))) for measurement models(((measurement model))) with any number of output quantities, both the ((GUM uncertainty framework)) and MCM(((Monte Carlo method))), as treated in <<JCGM1012008>>, require extension. The GUM <<JCGM1002008,clause=(GUM) F.1.2.3>> outlines such an extension of the ((GUM uncertainty framework)), but considers it further only in examples.


[[scls_7-5-2]]
==== {blank}

In JCGM 102 <<bipm_supp2>> it is stated that the ((law of propagation of uncertainty)), a main constituent of the ((GUM uncertainty framework)), can succinctly be expressed in an equivalent ((matrix form)) when applied to a ((measurement model)) having a single output quantity(((quantity,output))). The matrix expression has the advantage of being suitable as a basis for implementation in software(((software implementation))), and for extension to more general types of ((measurement model)).


==== {blank}

That extension is given in JCGM 102 for a measurement function having any number of output quantities. The extension to any number of output quantities(((quantity,output))) in a general ((measurement model))(((measurement model,with any number of output quantities))) (see <<scls_3-16>>) is also treated in JCGM 102.


==== {blank}

JCGM 102 also applies MCM(((Monte Carlo method))) to measurement models with any number of output quantities(((measurement model,with any number of output quantities))). A discrete representation of the ((probability distribution))(((probability distribution,discrete representation))) for the output quantities(((quantity,output))) is provided. Expressions are given for the ((estimate))s of the output quantities, the standard uncertainties(((standard uncertainty))) associated with these ((estimate))s, and the ((covariance))s associated with pairs of these ((estimate))s in terms of that representation.


==== {blank}

In addition to obtaining ((estimate))s of the output quantities, together with the associated standard uncertainties(((standard uncertainty))) and ((covariance))s, it might be required to obtain a _region_ containing the output quantities(((quantity,output))) with a specified (coverage) probability(((coverage probability))). It is natural to consider the extension to regions of the probabilistically symmetric coverage interval(((coverage interval,probabilistically symmetric))) and the shortest ((coverage interval)). However, there is no natural counterpart of a probabilistically symmetric ((coverage interval)) in the form of a ((coverage region)), whereas there is for a shortest coverage interval(((coverage interval,shortest))). The determination of a smallest coverage region(((coverage region,smallest))) is generally a difficult task.


==== {blank}

In some circumstances, it is reasonable to provide an _approximate_ coverage region(((coverage region,approximate))) having simple geometric shape. Two particular forms of ((coverage region)) are considered in this regard. One form results from characterizing the output quantities(((quantity,output))) by a joint Gaussian distribution(((probability distribution,joint Gaussian))), for example on the basis of the ((central limit theorem)) <<JCGM1002008,clause=(GUM) G.2>>, in which case the smallest coverage region(((coverage region,smallest))) is bounded by a ((hyper-ellipsoid)). The other form constitutes a hyper-rectangular coverage region(((coverage region,hyper-rectangular))). Procedures for obtaining these forms are provided in JCGM 102.
