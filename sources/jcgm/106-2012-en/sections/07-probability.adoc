
[[sec_7]]
== Probability of conformity with specified requirements

[[sec_7.1]]
=== General rule for calculation of conformance probability

[[sec_7.1.1]]
==== {blank}

An item conforms to a specified requirement if the true value of its associated
property stem:[Y] lies in the tolerance interval. Knowledge of stem:[Y]
is conveyed by a PDF stem:[g (eta | eta_{"m"})] so that a statement of
conformity is always an inference, with some probability of being true.
Denoting the set of permissible (conforming) values of stem:[Y] by stem:[C],
the conformance probability, denoted by stem:[p_{"c"}], is given by

[[eq_4]]
[stem]
++++
p_{"c"} = "Pr" (Y in sf(C) | eta_{"m"}) = int_{sf(C)} g (eta | eta_{"m"}) " d"eta.
++++

[[sec_7.1.2]]
==== {blank}

<<eq_4>> gives the general rule for calculating the probability that an
item conforms to a specified requirement based on measurement of a relevant
property of the item. Given a two-sided tolerance interval for the measurand
stem:[Y] , for example, with lower limit stem:[T_{"L"}] and upper limit
stem:[T_{"U"}], stem:[sf(C) = [T_{"L"}, T_{"U"}\]] and the conformance
probability is

[stem%unnumbered]
++++
p_{"c"} = int_{T_{"L"}}^{T_{"U"}} g (eta | eta_{"m"}) " d"eta.
++++

[[sec_7.1.3]]
==== {blank}

Since the item either does, or does not, conform to specification, the
probability that it does not conform is

[stem%unnumbered]
++++
bar(p_{"c"}) = 1 - p_{"c"}.
++++

[[sec_7.2]]
=== Conformance probabilities with normal PDFs

[[sec_7.2.1]]
==== {blank}

The conformance probability depends on a state of knowledge of a measurand
stem:[Y] as encoded and conveyed by the PDF stem:[g (eta | eta_{"m"})].
In many cases it is reasonable to characterise knowledge of stem:[Y] by
a {{normal distribution}} and this probability can be calculated. If the
prior distribution is normal and the measuring system (i.e., the likelihood
function) is characterised by a normal distribution then the distribution
stem:[g (eta | eta_{"m"})] is also a normal distribution.

[[sec_7.2.2]]
==== {blank}

More generally, if the likelihood function is characterised by a normal
distribution and the prior information is meagre, then the posterior
(post-measurement) PDF will be approximately normal. In such a case,
stem:[g (eta | eta_{"m"})] can be approximated adequately by a normal distribution
with expectation (mean) and standard deviation given by the best estimate
stem:[y] and standard uncertainty stem:[u] calculated as in clause <<sec_6.3.1>>.

NOTE: A normal distribution is completely specified by its expectation
(mean) and standard deviation.

NOTE: Some properties of normal PDFs are reviewed in <<annexA>>.

[[sec_7.2.3]]
==== {blank}

Because of their familiarity and widespread use, normal PDFs will be used
to illustrate the calculation of conformance probabilities in many examples
in this document. Such calculations can be extended to the case where a
small number of indications gives rise to a scaled and shifted __t__-distribution
(see <<JCGM_101_2008,clause=6.4.9>>).

[[sec_7.2.4]]
==== {blank}

Suppose that the PDF stem:[g (eta | eta_{"m"})] for the measurand stem:[Y]
is (or is well approximated by) a normal distribution specified by a best
estimate (expectation) y and standard uncertainty (standard deviation)
stem:[u]. Then stem:[g (eta | eta_{"m"})] is given by

[stem]
++++
g (eta | eta_{"m"}) = 1/{u sqrt(2pi)} exp [ -1/2 ({eta - y}/u)^2 ] " =: " varphi (eta; y, u^2).
++++

[[sec_7.2.5]]
==== {blank}

In general, the estimate stem:[y] depends on stem:[eta_{"m"}], i.e.,
stem:[y = y(eta_{"m"})]. When knowledge of stem:[Y] is meagre before the
measurement, then typically stem:[y ~~ eta_{"m"}]; see clause <<sec_A.4.4>>
for an example where this is not the case.

[[sec_7.2.6]]
==== {blank}

From the steps leading to <<eq_2>>, the probability that stem:[Y] lies
in the interval stem:[[a, b\]], given the PDF (5), is

[[eq_6]]
[stem]
++++
"Pr"(a <= Y <= b | eta_{"m"}) = phi ({b - y}/u) - phi ({a - y}/u),
++++

where stem:[y = y(eta_{"m"})] and

[stem%unnumbered]
++++
Phi(z) = 1/{sqrt(2pi)} int_{-oo}^{z} exp(-t^2//2)dt
++++

is the standard normal distribution function (see <<annexA>>).

[[sec_7.3]]
=== One-sided tolerance intervals with normal PDFs

[[sec_7.3.1]]
==== Single lower tolerance limit

<<fig3>> shows a one-sided tolerance interval with a single lower tolerance
limit stem:[T_{"L"}]. Conforming values of a property of interest stem:[Y]
lie in the interval stem:[eta >= T_{"L"}]. Knowledge of stem:[Y] following
an inspection measurement is conveyed by a normal PDF, shown superimposed
on the tolerance interval. The best estimate stem:[y] lies in the tolerance
interval; the shaded region to the left of stem:[T_{"L"}] indicates the
probability that the item does not conform with specification. From <<eq_6>>,
with stem:[a = T_{"L"}], stem:[b -> 1], and noting that
stem:[Phi(oo) = 1], the conformance probability is

[[eq_7]]
[stem]
++++
p_{"c"} = 1 - Phi ({T_{"L"} - y}/u).
++++

Since stem:[Phi(t) + Phi(-t) = 1], the probability (<<eq_7>>) can be written

[[eq_8]]
[stem]
++++
p_{"c"} = Phi ({y - T_{"L"}}/u).
++++

[[fig3]]
.Tolerance interval with a single lower tolerance limit stem:[T_{"L"}]. Knowledge of a quantity stem:[Y] (the measurable property of interest) following measurement is characterized by a normal PDF with best estimate stem:[y] and associated standard uncertainty stem:[u]. Conforming values of stem:[Y] lie in the interval stem:[eta >= T_{"L"}].
image::figure3.png[]

[[sec_7.3.2]]
==== Single upper tolerance limit

<<fig4>> shows a normal PDF superimposed on a one-sided tolerance interval
with a single upper tolerance limit stem:[T_{"U"}]. Conforming values of
a property of interest stem:[Y] lie in the interval stem:[eta <= T_{"U"}].
In this case, the shaded region to the right of stem:[T_{"U"}] indicates
the probability that the item does not conform with specification. From
<<eq_6>>, with stem:[a -> -oo], stem:[b = T_{"U"}], and noting that
stem:[Phi(-oo) = 0], the conformance probability is

[[eq_9]]
[stem]
++++
p_{"c"} = Phi ({T_{"U"} - y}/u).
++++

[[fig4]]
.As <<fig3>> except with a single upper tolerance limit stem:[T_{"U"}]. Conforming values of stem:[Y] lie in the interval stem:[eta <= T_{"U"}].
image::figure4.png[]

[[sec_7.3.3]]
==== General approach with single tolerance limits

Probabilities (<<eq_8>>) and (<<eq_9>>) are of the same form and can be
written as

[[eq_10]]
[stem]
++++
p_{"c"} = Phi(z),
++++

where stem:[z = (y - T_{"L"})//u] for a lower limit and
stem:[z = (T_{"U"} - y)//u] for an upper limit. In both cases, stem:[p_{"c"}]
is greater than or equal to 1/2 for an estimate stem:[y] in the tolerance
interval (stem:[z >= 0]) and less than 1/2 otherwise. <<table1>> shows
values of stem:[z] for several values of the conformance probability stem:[p_{"c"}].

[[table1]]
.Conformance (stem:[p_{"c"}]) and non-conformance (stem:[bar(p_{"c"}) = 1 - p_{"c"}]) probabilities for one-sided tolerance intervals and normal PDFs. For a lower limit, stem:[z = (y - T_{"L"})//u]; for an upper limit, stem:[z = (T_{"U"} - y)//u]. In both cases, stem:[z >= 0] for an estimate stem:[y] in the tolerance interval
|===
| stem:[p_{"c"}] | stem:[bar(p)_c] | stem:[z]
| 0.80 | 0.20 | 0.84
| 0.90 | 0.10 | 1.28
| 0.95 | 0.05 | 1.64
| 0.99 | 0.01 | 2.33
| 0.999 | 0.001 | 3.09
|===

[example]
The breakdown voltage stem:[V_{"b"}] of a Zener diode is measured, yielding
a best estimate stem:[v_{"b"} = -5.47 "unitsml(V)"] with associated standard
uncertainty stem:[u = 0.05 "unitsml(V)"]. Specification of the diode requires
stem:[V_{"b"} <= -5.40 "unitsml(V)"], which is an upper limit on the breakdown
voltage. Then stem:[z = [-5.40 - (-5.47)\]//0.05 = 1.40], and from <<eq_10>>,
stem:[p_{"c"} = Phi(1.40) = 0.92]. There is a 92 % probability that the
diode conforms to specification.

[example]
A metal container is destructively tested using pressurized water in a
measurement of its bursting strength stem:[B].
The measurement yields a best estimate stem:[b = 509.7 "unitsml(kPa)"],
with associated standard uncertainty stem:[u = 8.6 "unitsml(kPa)"]. The
container specification requires stem:[B >= 490 "unitsml(kPa)"], which
is a lower limit on the bursting strength. Then stem:[z = (509.7 - 490)//8.6 = 2.3]
and, from <<eq_10>>, stem:[p_{"c"} = Phi(2.3) = 0.99]. There is a 99 %
probability that the container conformed to specification prior to the
destructive test.

[[sec_7.4]]
=== Two-sided tolerance intervals with normal PDFs

<<fig5>> shows a two-sided tolerance interval with tolerance limits stem:[T_{"L"}]
and stem:[T_{"U"}] and length stem:[T = T_{"U"} - T_{"L"}] defining the
tolerance stem:[T]. As previously, knowledge of a measurand stem:[Y] is
assumed to be conveyed by a normal PDF. The estimate stem:[y] lies in the
tolerance interval and there is a visible fraction of the probability in
the region stem:[eta > T_{"U"}] beyond the upper tolerance limit.

[[fig5]]
.As <<fig3>> except with a two-sided tolerance interval. The length, stem:[T_{"U"} - T_{"L"}], of the interval is equal to the tolerance stem:[T]. Conforming values of stem:[Y] lie in the interval stem:[T_{"L"} <= eta <= T_{"U"}].
image::figure5.png[]

Using <<eq_6>> with stem:[b = T_{"U"}] and stem:[a = T_{"L"}] yields the
conformance probability

[[eq_11]]
[stem]
++++
p_{"c"} = Phi ({T_{"U"} - y}/u) - Phi({T_{"L"} - y}/u).
++++

[example]
A sample of SAE Grade 40 motor oil is required to have a kinematic viscosity
stem:[Y] at 100 °C of no less than stem:[12.5 "unitsml(mm^2/s)"] and no
greater than stem:[16.3 "unitsml(mm^2/s)"]. The kinematic viscosity of
the sample is measured at 100 °C, yielding a best estimate
stem:[y = 13.6 "unitsml(mm^2/s)"] and associated standard uncertainty
stem:[u = 1.8 "unitsml(mm^2/s)"]. Following <<eq_11>>, form the quantities

[stem%unnumbered]
++++
(T_{"U"} - y)//u = (16.3 - 13.6)//1.8 = 1.5, " " " " (T_{"L"} - y)//u = (12.5 - 13.6)//1.8 = -0.6,
++++

so that

[stem%unnumbered]
++++
p_{"c"} = Phi(1.5) - Phi(-0.6) = 0.93 - 0.27 = 0.66.
++++

There is a 66 % probability that the sample of motor oil conforms to specification.

[[sec_7.5]]
=== Conformance probability and coverage intervals

[[sec_7.5.1]]
==== {blank}

A measurement result can be summarized by giving a coverage interval with
an associated coverage probability (see clause <<sec_6.3.2>>) rather than
an explicit PDF for a measurand stem:[Y]. In such a case, a statement of
conformance probability can be made by comparing the coverage interval
with the tolerance interval. If a coverage interval with coverage probability
stem:[p] lies wholly within the tolerance interval, then stem:[p_{"c"}]
cannot be less than stem:[p]. This observation is illustrated in <<fig6>>,
which shows two 95 % coverage intervals near an upper tolerance limit.

[[sec_7.5.2]]
==== {blank}

Interval (a) extends beyond the tolerance limit and without knowing the
form of the PDF for stem:[Y], no definite statement can be made about the
conformance probability.

[[fig6]]
.Two 95 % coverage intervals for a measurand stem:[Y] near an upper tolerance limit stem:[T_{"U"}]. Interval (a) extends beyond the tolerance limit and the conformity cannot be decided without knowledge of the PDF for stem:[Y]. Interval (b) lies wholly within the tolerance interval; for this interval stem:[p_{"c"} >= 95" "%].
image::figure6.png[]

[[sec_7.5.3]]
==== {blank}

All values in interval (b) are less than the tolerance limit and there
are conforming values of stem:[Y] that do not belong to this interval,
so that stem:[p_{"c"} >= 95" "%].

[[sec_7.5.4]]
==== {blank}

In general, if stem:[[eta_{"low"}, eta_{"high"}\]] is a coverage interval
for stem:[Y], with coverage probability stem:[p], then

* for a single upper tolerance limit stem:[T_{"U"}], stem:[p_{"c"} >= p]
if stem:[eta_{"high"} <= T_{"U"}];

* for a single lower tolerance limit stem:[T_{"L"}], stem:[p_{"c"} >= p]
if stem:[eta_{"low"} >= T_{"L"}];

* for a two-sided interval with upper and lower tolerance limits stem:[T_{"U"}]
and stem:[T_{"L"}], stem:[p_{"c"} >= p] if stem:[eta_{"low"} >= T_{"L"}]
and stem:[eta_{"high"} <= T_{"U"}].

NOTE: Comparison of a coverage interval for a property of interest with
an interval of permissible values is the basis for deciding conformity
with specification as described in <<ref22>>.

NOTE: Given the PDF for stem:[Y], the conformance probability can always
be calculated. The PDF for a measurand is more informative than a coverage
interval with its associated coverage probability.

NOTE: When a conformity assessment of a measuring instrument is performed
-- particularly when the assessment is regulated by specific standards
-- the definition of the measurand, and consequently the uncertainty evaluation,
may not be straightforward and may require specific attention.

[[sec_7.6]]
=== Measurement capability index stem:[C_{"m"}]

[[sec_7.6.1]]
==== {blank}

Consider the case where prior information is so meagre that knowledge regarding
possible values of a measured property stem:[Y] can be considered to be
completely supplied by the measurement. In such a case, if the distribution
for stem:[Y] is assumed to be a normal PDF
stem:[g (eta | eta_{"m"}) = varphi (eta; y, u^2)], then stem:[y ~~ eta_{"m"}]
and stem:[u ~~ u_{"m"}] (see clause <<sec_A.4.4.3>>). In the following
subclauses it is assumed that this is the case, and a measurement result
will be summarised by the two parameters stem:[(eta_{"m"}, u_{"m"})], taken
to be the expectation and standard deviation of a normal PDF.

[[sec_7.6.2]]
==== {blank}

A parameter that characterises the quality of the measurement, relative
to a requirement specified by a tolerance, is called the {{measurement
capability index}} defined by

[[eq_12]]
[stem]
++++
C_{"m"} = {T_{"U"} - T_{"L"}}/{4 u_{"m"}} = T/{4 u_{"m"}} = T/{2U},
++++

where stem:[U = 2u_{"m"}] is the expanded uncertainty with coverage factor
stem:[k = 2].

[[sec_7.6.3]]
==== {blank}

The factor of 4 in <<eq_12>> is arbitrary; the particular choice is motivated
by the widespread use of the coverage interval
stem:[[eta_{"m"} - 2u_{"m"}, eta_{"m"} + 2u_{"m"}\]] in reporting the result
of a measurement. In the case where stem:[g (eta | eta_{"m"})] is a normal
PDF, the coverage probability for such an interval is approximately
95 %.

[[sec_7.6.4]]
==== {blank}

There is a close connection between stem:[C_{"m"}] and other derived parameters
that have been used to characterize measurement quality in various contexts.
Among these are gauging ratio, gauge maker's rule, test uncertainty ratio
(TUR) <<ref32>> and test accuracy ratio (TAR) <<ref1>>. Such parameters
are typically stated in ratio form such as a 10-to-1 rule or a TUR of 4:1.
Care has to be taken when such rules are encountered because they are sometimes
ambiguously or incompletely defined. Definition (12), on the other hand,
makes clear that a statement such as stem:[C_{"m"} >= 4] means that
stem:[u_{"m"} <= T//16].

[[sec_7.6.5]]
==== {blank}

In the calibration or verification of a measuring instrument, a specified
requirement is often expressed in terms of a maximum permissible error
(of indication) (see <<sec_3.3.18>>). Such a requirement means that when
the instrument is used to measure a quantity stem:[Y], the error of indication
must lie in an interval defined by specified upper and lower limits. In
the common case of a symmetric interval stem:[[-E_{"max"},E_{"max"}\]],
the tolerance is stem:[T = 2E_{"max"}] and the measurement capability
index is

[stem%unnumbered]
++++
C_{"m"} = {2E_{"max"}}/{2U} = {E_{"max"}}/U.
++++

In this expression, stem:[U] is the expanded uncertainty, for a coverage
factor stem:[k = 2], associated with a measurement of the error of indication
of the instrument.

[[sec_7.7]]
=== Measurement capability index and conformance probability

[[sec_7.7.1]]
==== {blank}

For a normal PDF, <<eq_11>> gives the conformance probability stem:[p_{"c"}]
in terms of a particular pair of tolerance limits stem:[(T_{"L"}, T_{"U"})]
and a particular measurement result summarized by stem:[(y, u)]. Taking
stem:[y = eta_{"m"}] and stem:[u = u_{"m"}], this expression can be re-written
in a form suitable for a general measurement problem by defining a quantity

[[eq_13]]
[stem]
++++
tilde(y) = {eta_{"m"} - T_{"L"}}/T .
++++

For an estimate stem:[eta_{"m"}] in the tolerance interval,
stem:[tilde y] lies in the interval stem:[0 <= tilde(y) <= 1].

[[sec_7.7.2]]
==== {blank}

For a normal post-measurement PDF stem:[varphi(eta; eta_{"m"}, u_{"m"}^2)],
<<eq_11>> can then be written, using <<eq_12>> and <<eq_13>>,

[[eq_14]]
[stem]
++++
p_{"c"} = Phi[4 C_{"m"}(1 - tilde(y))] - Phi(-4C_{"m"} tilde(y)) = p_{"c"} (tilde(y),C_{"m"}),
++++

so that stem:[p_{"c"}] is completely determined by the two quantities stem:[tilde(y)]
and stem:[C_{"m"}].

[[sec_7.7.3]]
==== {blank}

It is often the case that the standard uncertainty stem:[u_{"m"}] associated
with an estimate stem:[eta_{"m"}] has a fixed value that depends on the
design of the measuring system, but is independent of stem:[eta_{"m"}].
A series of water samples, for example, might be inspected to determine,
for each sample, a concentration of dissolved mercury, using a measurement
procedure that yields different estimates, each having the same associated
standard uncertainty stem:[u_{"m"}]. In such a case the measurement capability
index stem:[C_{"m"} = T//4u_{"m"}] is fixed, and a question as to whether
or not a measured property conforms to specification with an acceptable
probability can be decided based on the estimate stem:[eta_{"m"}], using
<<eq_13>> and <<eq_14>> with stem:[C_{"m"}] fixed.

NOTE: A case where the standard uncertainty stem:[u_{"m"}] is proportional
to the estimate stem:[eta_{"m"}] is treated in <<ref13,clause=A>>.

The curve separates regions of conformity and non-conformity at a 95 % level of confidence. The post-measurement distribution for the measurand stem:[Y] is taken to be the normal PDF stem:[varphi(eta; eta_{"m"}, u_{"m"}^2)].

[[sec_7.7.4]]
==== {blank}

There is an infinite number of pairs stem:[(tilde(y),C_{"m"})] that yield
a given conformance probability stem:[p_{"c"}] via <<eq_14>>. <<fig7>>
shows stem:[C_{"m"}] versus stem:[tilde(y)] along a curve of constant
95 % conformance probability for estimates stem:[eta_{"m"}] within the
tolerance interval stem:[0 <= tilde(y) <= 1]. The curve separates regions
of conformity (unshaded) and non-conformity (shaded) at a 95 % level of
confidence.

[[sec_7.7.5]]
==== {blank}

The horizontal axis in <<fig7>> corresponds to stem:[C_{"m"} = 1], or
stem:[u_{"m"} = T//4]. For this relatively large standard uncertainty it
can be seen that stem:[p_{"c"} >= 95" "%] only for
stem:[0.45 <= tilde(y) <= 0.55]. If the measured property was required
to conform to specification with at least a 95 % level of confidence, an
acceptable estimate stem:[eta_{"m"}] would thus have to lie in the central
approximately 10 % of the tolerance interval.
