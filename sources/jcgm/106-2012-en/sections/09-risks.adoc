
[[sec_9]]
== Consumer's and producer's risks

[[sec_9.1]]
=== General

[[sec_9.1.1]]
==== {blank}

In conformity assessment using a binary decision rule, a property of an
item is measured and the item is accepted as conforming if the measured
value of the property lies in a defined acceptance interval. A measured
value outside the acceptance interval leads to rejection of the item as
non-conforming. <<fig12>>, which reproduces <<fig1>>, illustrates the intervals
of interest, showing a tolerance interval (of conforming values) and an
acceptance interval (of permissible measured values).

[[sec_9.1.2]]
==== {blank}

The use of guard bands provides a way to limit the probability of making
an incorrect conformance decision based on measurement information as summarised
by a coverage interval. The present clause is concerned with a more precise
evaluation of such probabilities for a production process. The evaluated
probabilities depend on two factors, the production process and the measuring
system.

[[sec_9.1.3]]
==== {blank}

If the measuring system were perfectly accurate, all conformance decisions
would be correct and all risks would be zero. An increase in measurement
uncertainty means an increase in the probability of an incorrect decision,
and the probability is greatest when measured values are close to the tolerance
limits.

[[sec_9.1.4]]
==== {blank}

The risks also depend on the nature of the production process. If the process
rarely produces an item whose property of interest is near the tolerance
limits, there is less opportunity for incorrect decisions to be made. Conversely,
if a process produces items with properties likely to be close to tolerance
limits, the uncertainties associated with the measurements are brought
into play. The remainder of this clause shows how the contributions of
both factors can be evaluated.

[[fig12]]
.Binary conformity assessment where decisions are based on measured quantity values. The true value of a measurable property (the measurand) of an item is specified to lie in a tolerance interval defined by limits stem:[(T_{"L"}, T_{"U"})]. The item is accepted as conforming if the measured value of the property lies in an acceptance interval defined by limits stem:[(A_{"L"}, A_{"U"})], and rejected as non-conforming otherwise.

[[sec_9.2]]
=== PDFs for production process and measuring system

[[sec_9.2.1]]
==== {blank}

Consider a process that produces a sequence of items, each having a measurable
property stem:[Y] with possible values stem:[eta]. The process might be
a machine producing resistors of nominal resistance stem:[10 "unitsml(kOhm)"]
or a sampling process yielding vials of ocean water containing dissolved
mercury. For an item chosen at random from the process, knowledge of property
stem:[Y] before it is measured is conveyed by a prior PDF stem:[g_0 (eta)].
The PDF stem:[g_0 (eta)] can be said to characterize the production process,
and is sometimes called the process probability density. The form of
stem:[g_0 (eta)] is typically assigned based on knowledge acquired by measuring
the property of interest in a sample of produced items.

NOTE: Assigning a prior PDF based on a measured sample of items is discussed
in <<annexB>>.

[[sec_9.2.2]]
==== {blank}

Conformity assessment of a produced item is realized by a measurement of
the property of interest. The output of the measuring system is a quantity
regarded as an observable random variable stem:[Y_{"m"}] whose possible
values stem:[eta_{"m"}], assuming a known input value stem:[Y = eta], are
encoded and conveyed by a PDF stem:[h (eta_{"m"} | eta)]. The form of
stem:[h (eta_{"m"} | eta)] is assigned based on the design of the measuring
system, information supplied by calibrations and knowledge of relevant
influence quantities such as environmental parameters and material properties.

[[sec_9.3]]
=== Possible outcomes of an inspection measurement with a binary decision rule

[[sec_9.3.1]]
==== {blank}

Let stem:[sf(C)] and stem:[tilde sf(C)] denote, respectively, intervals
of conforming and non-conforming values of stem:[Y]; and let stem:[sf(A)]
and stem:[tilde sf(A)]
denote, respectively, intervals of acceptable and non-acceptable values
of stem:[Y_{"m"}]. In <<fig12>>, for example, stem:[sf(C)] corresponds
to values of stem:[Y] in the interval stem:[T_{"L"} <= Y <= T_{"U"}], and
stem:[A] corresponds to values of stem:[Y_{"m"}] in the interval
stem:[A_{"L"} <= Y_{"m"} <= A_{"U"}].

[[sec_9.3.2]]
==== {blank}

With a binary decision rule there are four possible outcomes of a conformity
assessment test that yields a measured quantity value stem:[eta_{"m"}]:

*Valid acceptance:* the item is accepted stem:[(Y_{"m"} = eta_{"m"} in sf(A))]
and conforms with specification stem:[(Y in sf(C))]. This is a desired
outcome of the conformity assessment test, leading to acceptance of a conforming
item.

*False acceptance:* the item is accepted stem:[(Y_{"m"} = eta_{"m"} in sf(A))]
but does not conform with specification stem:[(Y in tilde(sf(C)))]. This
is an incorrect decision whose probability is called consumer's risk, because
the cost associated with such a mistake is often borne by a consumer, or
user, who accepts the item as fit for its purpose and acts accordingly.

NOTE: False acceptance is also known as a pass error or a false positive.

For a particular measured item accepted as conforming, given a measured
value stem:[Y_{"m"} = eta_{"m"} in sf(A)], the probability of false acceptance
is called the {{specific consumer's risk}} <<ref38>>, denoted by stem:[R_{"C"}^{**}].

From the definition (<<eq_4>>) of conformance probability it can be seen
that stem:[R_{"C"}^{**}] is given by

[stem%unnumbered]
++++
R_{"C"}^{**} = 1 - p_{"c"},
++++

for a measured value stem:[eta_{"m"}] in the acceptance interval. For an
item chosen at random from the production process, the probability that
it will be falsely accepted following a measurement is called the
{{global consumer's risk}} <<ref38>>, denoted by stem:[R_C]. Calculation
of stem:[R_C] is considered in clause <<sec_9.5>>.

*Valid rejection:* the item is rejected stem:[(Y_{"m"} = eta_{"m"} in tilde(sf(A)))]
and does not conform with specification stem:[(Y in tilde(sf(C)))]. This
is a desired outcome of the conformity assessment test, leading to rejection
of a non-conforming item.

*False rejection:* the item is rejected stem:[(Y_{"m"} = eta_{"m"} in tilde(sf(A)))]
but actually conforms with specification stem:[(Y in sf(C))]. This is another
incorrect decision whose probability is called producer's risk, because
the cost associated with such a mistake is often borne by a producer who
cannot sell an item that has failed a test of conformity.

NOTE: False rejection is also known as a fail error or a false negative.

For a particular measured item rejected as non-conforming, given a measured
value stem:[Y_{"m"} = eta_{"m"} in tilde(sf(A))], the probability of false
rejection is called the {{specific producer's risk}} <<ref38>>, denoted
by stem:[R_{"P"}^{**}]. From the definition (<<eq_4>>) of conformance probability
it can be seen that stem:[R_{"P"}^{**}] is given by

[stem%unnumbered]
++++
R_{"P"}^{**} = p_{"c"},
++++

for a measured value stem:[eta_{"m"}] outside the acceptance interval.
For an item chosen at random from the production process, the probability
that it will be falsely rejected following a measurement is called the
{{global producer's risk}} <<ref38>>, denoted stem:[R_P]. Calculation of
stem:[R_P] is considered in clause <<sec_9.5>>.

[[sec_9.4]]
=== The joint PDF for stem:[Y] and stem:[Y_{"m"}]

[[sec_9.4.1]]
==== {blank}

As seen in clause <<sec_9.3.2>>, the specific consumer's risk and specific
producer's risk stem:[R_{"C"}^{**}]
and stem:[R_{"P"}^{**}] are simply related to the conformance probability
for a particular measured item, given the result of a measurement. If the
value of the property stem:[Y] is outside the tolerance interval and a
measured value stem:[Y_{"m"}] is within the acceptance interval, then a
consumer's risk has been realised. The probability that these two events
occur, i.e., the global consumer's risk, is specified by a joint probability
distribution that depends on the production process and the measuring system.

[[sec_9.4.2]]
==== {blank}

The joint probability density can be written as a product of densities
that are already known. In words, the probability that the value of the
measurand stem:[Y] is outside the tolerance interval and measured value
stem:[Y_{"m"}] is within the acceptance interval is given by the probability
that the production process produces an item with a true value of stem:[Y]
outside the tolerance interval multiplied by the probability that the measuring
system produces a measured value stem:[Y_{"m"}] within the acceptance interval,
given that the measurand stem:[Y] is outside the tolerance interval.

[[sec_9.4.3]]
==== {blank}

Similarly, the global producer's risk is defined in terms of the same joint
probability distribution. If the tolerance interval, the production process
and the measuring system are regarded as fixed, the global consumer's risk
and global producer's risk are determined by the acceptance limits. The
acceptance limits can thus be set to achieve an acceptable balance of the
two kinds of risk. In general it is not possible to set the acceptance
limits to minimise both the consumer's and producer's risks simultaneously:
decreasing one will increase the other.

[[sec_9.4.4]]
==== {blank}

In the quality control and conformity assessment literature, the terms
'consumer's risk' and 'producer's risk'
are generally used in the sense of global risks, as described above.

[[sec_9.4.5]]
==== {blank}

For a given production process and measuring system, knowledge of the possible
outcomes of a conformity assessment test of an item selected at random
is described by a joint probability density function. For such a randomly
selected item, the probability that (a) the value of the measurand stem:[Y]
lies in the interval stem:[eta <= Y <= eta + "d"eta] and that (b) a measurement
of stem:[Y] would yield a measured value stem:[Y_{"m"}] in the interval
stem:[eta_{"m"} <= Y_{"m"} <= eta_{"m"} + "d"eta_{"m"}] is given by

[[eq_15]]
[stem]
++++
"Pr" (eta <= Y <= eta + "d"eta " and " eta_{"m"} <= Y_{"m"} <= eta_{"m"} + "d"eta_{"m"}) = f(eta, eta_{"m"})"d"eta "d"eta_{"m"},
++++

where stem:[f(eta, eta_{"m"})] is the joint PDF for stem:[Y] and stem:[Y_{"m"}].

[[sec_9.4.6]]
==== {blank}

Using the product (or multiplication) rule of probability theory, the joint
density stem:[f(eta, eta_{"m"})] can be factorized in two ways, according
to 

[[eq_16a]]
[stem]
++++
sf(eta, eta_{"m"}) = g_0 (eta) h (eta_{"m"} | eta)
++++

and

[[eq_16b]]
[stem]
++++
f(eta; eta_{"m"}) = h_0 (eta_{"m"}) g (eta | eta_{"m"}) .
++++

[[sec_9.4.7]]
==== {blank}

The two PDFs on the right in <<eq_16a>> are the two probability densities
described in clause <<sec_9.2>>. Given the forms of these PDFs, the two
probability densities on the right-hand side of <<eq_16b>> can be calculated,
if desired. Such a calculation is illustrated in <<annexA>> (see clauses
<<sec_A.4.3>> and <<sec_A.4.4>>).

[[sec_9.5]]
=== Calculation of global risks

[[sec_9.5.1]]
==== Historical context

[[sec_9.5.1.1]]
===== {blank}

In the following subclauses, formulae are developed for calculating the
global risks of incorrect decisions. Such calculations have traditionally
been performed using measured frequency distributions of the various outcomes
when a large sample of nominally identical items are measured. The global
consumer's risk, in such an approach, is equal to the fraction of items
in a measured sample that are accepted for use but do not conform with
a specified requirement. Such non-conformance, for a particular item, has
to be demonstrated after the fact by a separate measurement with an uncertainty
much smaller than that of the measuring system used in the conformity assessment.

[[sec_9.5.1.2]]
===== {blank}

The global risks below are calculated using probability distributions rather
than frequency distributions, so that it is not necessary to consider an
ensemble of measured items that may not, in fact, exist. Numerically, the
calculated probabilities will always agree, on average, with measured frequencies.
Thus acceptance limits can be chosen to yield acceptable fractions of mistakenly
accepted or rejected items, on average, in the conformity assessment of
items in a sample.

[[sec_9.5.2]]
==== General formulae

[[sec_9.5.2.1]]
===== {blank}

Given the joint PDF (<<eq_16a>>) and the two probability densities
stem:[g_0 (eta)] and stem:[h (eta_{"m"} | eta)], the probabilities of each
of the four possible outcomes described above (see clause <<sec_9.3>>)
can be calculated. These probabilities are simply the respective volumes
under the joint probability density stem:[f(eta, eta_{"m"})], integrated
over the four regions that describe all possible outcomes.

[[sec_9.5.2.2]]
===== {blank}

Of particular interest are the global consumer's risk and global producer's
risk, calculated as follows:

* For a measured value in the acceptance interval and a value of stem:[Y]
outside the tolerance interval, the global consumer's risk is
+
--
[[eq_17]]
[stem]
++++
R_C = int_{tilde(sf(C))} int_{sf(A)} g_0 (eta) h (eta_{"m"} | eta) "d"eta_{"m"} "d"eta
++++
--

* For a measured value outside the acceptance interval and a value of stem:[Y]
within the tolerance interval, the global producer's risk is
+
--
[[eq_18]]
[stem]
++++
R_P = int_{sf(C)} int_{tilde(sf(A))} g_0 (eta) h (eta_{"m"} | eta) "d"eta_{"m"} "d"eta .
++++
--

[[sec_9.5.2.3]]
===== {blank}

<<eq_17>> and <<eq_18>> are general formulae for the calculation of global
consumer's and producer's risks. Depending on the particular form of the
PDFs stem:[g_0 (eta)] and stem:[h (eta_{"m"} | eta)], explicit evaluation
of stem:[R_C] and stem:[R_P] may have to be performed numerically.

[[sec_9.5.3]]
==== Special case: Binary decision rule

[[sec_9.5.3.1]]
===== {blank}

For the particular binary conformity assessment illustrated in <<fig12>>,
formulae (<<eq_17>>) and (<<eq_18>>) become

[[eq_19]]
[stem]
++++
R_C = (int_{-oo}^{T_{"L"}} int_{T_{"U"}}^{oo}) int_{A_{"L"}}^{A_{"U"}} g_0 (eta) h (eta_{"m"} | eta) "d"eta_{"m"} "d"eta ,
++++

and

[[eq_20]]
[stem]
++++
R_P = (int_{-oo}^{A_{"L"}} int_{A_{"U"}}^{oo}) int_{T_{"L"}}^{T_{"U"}} g_0 (eta) h (eta_{"m"} | eta) "d"eta "d"eta_{"m"} .
++++

[[sec_9.5.3.2]]
===== {blank}

Use of <<eq_19>> and <<eq_20>> in the case where the joint PDF (<<eq_15>>)
is a product of normal distributions is illustrated in the following example.
Properties of normal distributions, including the particular forms of <<eq_19>>
and <<eq_20>>, are discussed in <<annexA>>.

.Manufacture of precision resistors
====
A supplier of electrical components produces wire-wound precision resistors
of nominal resistance stem:[1" "500 "unitsml(Ohm)"]. For each resistor
(the item), the resistance stem:[Y] (the property of interest) is specified
to lie in a tolerance interval defined by the limits
stem:[T_{"L"} = 1499.8  "unitsml(Ohm)"] and stem:[T_{"U"} = 1 500.2 "unitsml(Ohm)"]:

A machine for producing such resistors is evaluated by measuring a sample
of its output, using a high-accuracy ohmmeter with negligible measurement
uncertainty. A histogram of the measured values appears normal in shape,
centred on the nominal value with a standard deviation
stem:[sigma = 0.12 "unitsml(Ohm)"]. Based on this information, a normal
PDF stem:[g_0 (eta) = varphi (eta; y_0; u_0^2)] is assigned to model the
production process, with stem:[y_0 = 1500  "unitsml(Ohm)"] and
stem:[u_0 = sigma = 0.12  "unitsml(Ohm)"].

For a typical resistor produced by this machine, the conformance
probability is

[[eq_21]]
[stem]
++++
p_{"c"} =  int_{T_{"L"}}^{T_{"U"}} g_0 (eta) "d"eta = int_{1499.8}^{1500.2} varphi (eta; 1500, 0.12^2) "d" eta ~~ 0.90 = 90% .
++++

If the supplier simply shipped every resistor produced, about 10 % of them
would be non-conforming, which for economic reasons is judged to be unacceptable.
By purchasing a more expensive production machine the process variability
could be reduced. In this case it is decided, considering the relative
costs, to keep the existing machine and implement an inspection process
to detect and remove non-conforming resistors.

In production, the resistors are inspected for conformity with specification
using a calibrated high-speed ohmmeter. A normal PDF
stem:[h (eta_{"m"} | eta) = varphi (eta_{"m"}; eta, u_{"m"}^2)],
with stem:[u_{"m"} = 0.04 "unitsml(Ohm)"], is assigned to encode and convey
belief in the interval of measured values that might be observed when measuring
a known resistance stem:[Y = eta]. The assignment is based on a model of
the measuring system and an evaluation of the measurement uncertainty,
including the uncertainty associated with the ohmmeter calibration.

In order to reduce the probability of shipping resistors that do not meet
specification (the consumer's risk), acceptance limits
stem:[A_{"L"} = 1 499.82 "unitsml(Ohm)"], stem:[A_{"U"} = 1 500.18 "unitsml(Ohm)"]
are chosen inside the tolerance interval(see <<fig12>>), creating a guarded
acceptance interval with symmetric guard bands of length

[stem%unnumbered]
++++
w = (1 500.2 - 1 500.18) "unitsml(Ohm)" = 0.02 "unitsml(Ohm)" = 0.25U .
++++

The consumer's and producer's risks are then calculated from <<eq_A.15;to!eq_A.17>>
with

[stem%unnumbered]
++++
varphi_0 (z) = (1//sqrt(2pi)) exp(-z^2//2)
++++

and

[stem%unnumbered]
++++
F(z) = Phi ({A_{"U"} - y_0 - u_0z}/{u_{"m"}}) - Phi ({A_{"L"} - y_0 - u_0z}/{u_{"m"}}) = Phi (4.5 - 3z) - Phi(-4.5 - 3z) .
++++

Numerical integration yields

[stem%unnumbered]
++++
R_C = int_{-oo}^{-1.667} F(z) varphi_0(z) "d"z + int_{1.667}^{oo} F(z)varphi_0(z)"d"z = 0.01 = 1% ,
++++

and

[stem%unnumbered]
++++
R_P = int_{-1.667}^{1.667} [1 - F(z)] varphi_0(z) "d"z = 0.07 = 7 % .
++++

Interesting features of this conformity assessment procedure can be noted
by considering an average sample of 100 resistors produced by the machine,
measured, and accepted or rejected as suitable for use:

* given the properties of the production process, 90 of the resistors conform
with specification and 10 do not conform (see <<eq_21>>);

* of the 90 conforming resistors, 83 are accepted and 7 falsely rejected
as non-conforming;

* of the 10 non-conforming resistors, 9 are rejected and one falsely accepted
as conforming;

* 84 of the resistors are accepted; of these, stem:[83//84 ~~ 99 %] conform,
with about 1 % out of tolerance. This is the goal of the inspection measurement,
reducing the proportion of non-conforming resistors, among those accepted
for use, from 10 % to 1 %;

* of the 16 resistors that are rejected, stem:[7//16 ~~ 44 %] actually
conform with specification. This is a price to be paid for reducing the
risk of accepting non-conforming products.
====

[[sec_9.5.4]]
==== Setting acceptance limits

[[sec_9.5.4.1]]
===== {blank}

In the example above, the global risks stem:[R_C] and stem:[R_P] were calculated
given known acceptance limits stem:[A_{"L"}] and stem:[A_{"U"}]. In most
real applications, a desired level of risk is chosen based on a cost analysis
and acceptance limits are calculated so as to ensure that the desired risk
level is achieved. Such calculations are not straightforward. A practical
approach to such problems is via graphical solution, as illustrated in
the following example.

.Ball bearing production
====
A manufacturer produces large numbers of precision ball bearings. The performance
specification for these bearings (the items) requires that, for each of
them, the radial error motion (the property of interest) be less than
stem:[2 "unitsml(um)"]. Radial error motion of a bearing is undesired motion
perpendicular to the axis of rotation. For a perfect bearing, the radial
error motion would be zero; any real bearing will have a positive radial
error motion.

In order to characterize the production process, the radial error motions
of a large sample of bearings are measured, using a high-accuracy test
apparatus with negligible measurement uncertainty. For this sample, the
average observed radial error motion is stem:[bar(y) = 1 "unitsml(um)"],
with an associated sample standard deviation stem:[s = 0.5 "unitsml(um)"]:

Prior to shipment, bearings are tested for conformity with specification.
In these tests the radial error motion is measured using a calibrated test
apparatus. The measuring system is characterized by a normal PDF
stem:[varphi (eta_{"m"}; eta, eta_{"m"}^2)]  with a standard uncertainty
of stem:[u_{"m"} = 0.25 "unitsml(um)"].

For economic reasons, the fraction of non-conforming bearings sold to customers
as conforming (the global consumer's risk) must be held to 0.1 % or less.
How can an acceptance limit A be chosen to satisfy this requirement?

// [[fig13]]
.Tolerance and acceptance intervals for the conformity assessment of ball bearings. Permissible values of radial error motion stem:[Y] lie in the interval stem:[0 <= eta <= T]. The acceptance limit stem:[A] is separated from the tolerance limit stem:[T] by a guard band with length parameter stem:[w = rU = 2ru_{"m"}]. The decision rule in this case is guarded acceptance, with stem:[w > 0].
image::figure13.png[]

The conformity assessment problem is illustrated in <<fig13>>. A conforming
ball bearing is specified to have a radial error motion stem:[Y] in the
interval stem:[0 <= eta <= T]. Since the radial error motion is always
positive, with measured values close to zero, the prior PDF for the radial
error stem:[Y] will be modelled by a gamma probability density (see <<annexB>>,
clause <<sec_B.3>>). Based on the sample measurements, the expectation
and standard uncertainty of the prior PDF are assigned to be
stem:[y_0 = y = 1 "unitsml(um)"] and stem:[u_0 = s = 0.5 "unitsml(um)"].
Using <<eq_B.14>>, the parameters stem:[alpha] and stem:[lambda] are calculated:

[stem%unnumbered]
++++
alpha = {1^2}/{(0.5)^2} = 4, " " " " lambda = 1/{(0.5)^2} = 4 .
++++

From definition (<<eq_B.11>>) of the gamma probability density, the prior
PDF for bearing radial error stem:[Y] is then

[[eq_22]]
[stem]
++++
g_0 (eta) = "gamma" (eta ; 4, 4) = 128/3 eta^3 e^{-4 eta} , " " eta >= 0 .
++++

// [[fig14]]
.Gamma prior PDF given by <<eq_22>>, assigned based on the frequency distribution of measured radial error motions for a sample of ball bearings. The tolerance interval is the region stem:[0 <= eta <= 2 "unitsml(um)"]. The expectation of the distribution is the prior estimate stem:[y_0 = 1 "unitsml(um)"], with an associated standard uncertainty stem:[u_0 = 0.5 "unitsml(um)"]. Because the distribution is not symmetric, the most probable value of stem:[Y] (the mode of the distribution, here equal to stem:[0.75 "unitsml(um)"]) is not equal to stem:[y_0].
image::figure14.png[]

This PDF is shown in <<fig14>>. The probability that a ball bearing chosen
at random from the production process would
display a radial error motion greater than stem:[2 "unitsml(um)"] is indicated
by the cross-hatched region. This non-conformance probability is

[stem%unnumbered]
++++
bar(p_{"c"}) = int_{2}^{oo} "gamma" (eta; 4, 4) "d"eta = 0.042 ,
++++

which means that if all ball bearings produced were shipped without being
measured, about 4.2 % of them would be non-
conforming. The post-process measuring system is designed to detect non-conforming
bearings so that they will not be shipped. An acceptance limit is desired
to reduce the consumer's risk stem:[R_C] to 0.1 % or less. For the conformity
assessment decision rule shown in <<fig13>>, the tolerance interval corresponds
to stem:[0 <= Y <= T] and the acceptance interval to
stem:[0 <= Y_{"m"} <= A]. In a manner analogous to the steps leading to
<<eq_19>> and <<eq_20>>, the global consumer's and producer's risks are
evaluated as

[stem%unnumbered]
++++
R_C = int_{T}^{oo} int_{0}^{A} g_0 (eta) h (eta_{"m"} | eta) "d"eta_{"m"} "d"eta , " " " " R_P = int_{0}^{T} int_{A}^{oo} g_0 (eta) h (eta_{"m"} | eta) "d"eta_{"m"} "d"eta .
++++

For a measuring system characterized by a normal PDF
stem:[h (eta_{"m"} | eta) = varphi (eta_{"m"}; eta, eta_{"m"}^2)], making
the substitutions stem:[z = (eta_{"m"} - eta)//u_{"m"}, "d"z = "d"eta_{"m"}//u_{"m"}],
and performing the integrations over stem:[z], these expressions become

[stem%unnumbered]
++++
R_C = int_{T}^{oo} [Phi ({A - eta}/{u_{"m"}}) - Phi (- eta/{eta_{"m"}})] g_0 (eta) "d"eta , " " R_P = int_{0}^{T} [1 - Phi ({A - eta}/{eta_{"m"}})] g_0 (eta) "d"eta .
++++

From <<fig13>> it can be seen that stem:[A = T - 2r u_{"m"}]. Here stem:[T = 2 "unitsml(um)"] and stem:[u_{"m"} = 0.25 "unitsml(um)"]. Then setting stem:[g_0 (eta)] equal to the gamma PDF of <<eq_22>> yields the explicit results

[[eq_23]]
[stem]
++++
R_C(r) = 128/3 int_2^{oo} [Phi (8 - 2r - 4eta) - Phi(-4eta)] eta^3 e^{-4 eta} "d"eta ,
++++

[[eq_24]]
[stem]
++++
R_P(r) = 128/3 int_0^2 [1 - Phi(8 - 2r - 4eta)] eta^3 e^{-4eta} "d"eta .
++++

// [[fig15]]
.Global consumer's risk stem:[R_C] versus guard band multiplier stem:[r]. For stem:[r ~~ 0.65], the acceptance limit is stem:[A = T - 2(0.65)u_{"m"} = 1.7 "unitsml(um)"], and the desired risk stem:[R_C = 0.1 %] is achieved.
image::figure15.png[]

These integrals cannot be evaluated in closed form, but they can be calculated
numerically for any chosen values of the guard band multiplier stem:[r].

<<fig15>> shows the global consumer's risk stem:[R_C] for
stem:[-1 <= r <= 1]. Positive stem:[r] corresponds to stem:[A < T] (guarded
acceptance) and negative r corresponds to stem:[A > T]: For stem:[r = 0]
there is no guard band stem:[(A = T)], a decision rule called shared risk
or simple acceptance (see clause <<sec_8.2>>). The figure shows that the
desired level of risk, stem:[R_C = 0.1 %], is achieved for a guard band
multiplier stem:[r ~~ 0.65]. This results in a guarded acceptance interval
with acceptance limit

[stem%unnumbered]
++++
A = T - 2ru_{"m"} = (2 - 2 times 0.65 times 0.25) "unitsml(um)" ~~ 1.7 "unitsml(um)" .
++++

This choice of acceptance limit solves the decision problem.

In conformity assessment with a binary decision rule, acting to reduce
the consumer's risk will always increase the producer's risk. This general
rule is well illustrated by <<fig16>> which shows stem:[R_P] versus stem:[R_C],
calculated numerically from formulae (<<eq_23>>) and (<<eq_24>>), for the
ball bearing example. For stem:[r = 0.65]; the global producer's risk stem:[R_P]
is about 7.5 %. This means that about 75 of every 1000 ball bearings that
fail inspection would actually conform with specification, resulting in
the loss of revenue that would accrue if these good bearings were sold.

The generation of an increasing amount of conforming scrap is a cost of
guarded acceptance, which seeks to reduce the acceptance and shipment of
non-conforming products. In practice a supplier must choose an operating
point along a curve such as that shown in <<fig16>> that will balance the
risks and yield an optimal outcome. The choice of such an operating point
is a business or policy decision that requires an economic analysis of
the decision problem.

[[sec_9.5.5]]
==== General graphical approach

[[sec_9.5.5.1]]
===== {blank}

For a process with given tolerance T, normal prior PDF
stem:[g_0 (eta) = varphi (eta; y_0; u_0^2)] and normal measuring system
PDF stem:[h (eta_{"m"} | eta) = varphi (eta_{"m"}; eta, u_{"m"}^2)], a
graph such as that shown in <<fig17>> can be created to aid in setting
acceptance limits.

[[sec_9.5.5.2]]
===== {blank}

In this example it is assumed that prior information is meagre in the sense
that stem:[u_{"m"}^2" << "u_0^2], so that the
estimate stem:[y ~~ eta_{"m"}] with associated standard uncertainty
stem:[u ~~ u_{"m"}] (see clause <<sec_A.4.4.3>>).

[[sec_9.5.5.3]]
===== {blank}

The figure shows stem:[R_P] versus stem:[R_C] for the particular case where
stem:[u_0 = T//6].

[[sec_9.5.5.4]]
===== {blank}

The five curves in the figure correspond to values of the measurement capability
index stem:[C_{"m"} = T//(4u_{"m"})] in a range from 2 to 10, and along
each curve solid points locate guard bands with various length parameters,
from stem:[w = -U] to stem:[w = U], with the expanded uncertainty stem:[U]
equal to stem:[2u].

[[sec_9.5.5.5]]
===== {blank}

To use this particular graph it would be necessary to note that

* the process is assumed to be centred, so that the prior estimate stem:[y_0]
of the measurand lies at the midpoint of the tolerance interval;

* the upper and lower guard bands are assumed to have length parameters
that are equal in absolute value (a symmetric acceptance interval);

* stem:[R_P] and stem:[R_C] are calculated assuming normal process and
measuring system PDFs;

* for measurement capabilities other than the five values shown, interpolation
is possible;

* it is also possible to interpolate along the curves to estimate the guard
bands.

[[sec_9.5.6]]
==== The value of reduced measurement uncertainty

[[sec_9.5.6.1]]
===== {blank}

Reducing the uncertainty associated with the result a of conformity assessment
measurement will also reduce the probability of making an incorrect accept/reject
decision. This observation is well illustrated in <<fig17>> by the dotted
lines that mark the loci of the various guard bands.

[[sec_9.5.6.2]]
===== {blank}

For a simple acceptance decision rule stem:[(w = 0)] it is seen, for example,
that if the measurement uncertainty were such that
stem:[C_{"m"} = T//(4u_{"m"}) = 2], then the consumer's risk would be
stem:[R_C ~~ 0.1 %] and the corresponding producer's risk would be
stem:[R_P ~~ 1.5 %].

[[sec_9.5.6.3]]
===== {blank}

Investing in an improved measuring system with stem:[C_{"m"} = 10] would
reduce these risks to stem:[R_C ~~ 0.04 %] and stem:[R_P ~~ 0.07 %] respectively.
Whether such a reduction in measurement uncertainty would be economically
desirable depends on the tradeoff between the cost of the improved metrology
and the money saved via fewer decision mistakes.

[[sec_9.5.6.4]]
===== {blank}

Improving the production process (reducing the prior standard uncertainty
stem:[u_0]) will have a similar effect in reducing both consumer's and
producer's risks, and will involve a similar kind of cost/benefit analysis.
