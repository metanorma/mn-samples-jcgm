== Prior knowledge of the measurand

[[sec_B.1]]
=== Statistical process control

[[sec_B.1.1]]
==== {blank}

In many conformity assessments, knowledge of a measurand stem:[Y] prior
to making a measurement is not explicitly considered in making accept/reject
decisions. In such cases, which are typical of measurements analyzed according
to principles described in the GUM, there is an implicit assumption that
prior knowledge of stem:[Y] is so meagre as to have a negligible effect
on the outcome of the decision.

[[sec_B.1.2]]
==== {blank}

For a process in which a number of nominally identical items are produced
over time, the nature of the process can be studied by periodically measuring
a sample of its output. Statistics generated in the course of such measurements,
such as a moving sample average and a sample standard deviation, provide
information about the stability of the process so that it can be adjusted
as necessary to meet production quality criteria. The generation and use
of measurement information in this way forms the basis of statistical process
control (SPC). A sizable literature is available; see for example references
<<ref33>>, <<ref43>>.

[[sec_B.1.3]]
==== {blank}

The behaviour of a process in SPC is commonly summarized by assuming that
a sample of items measured for quality control purposes comprises a set
of realizations of a stable frequency distribution. The mean value stem:[mu]
and standard deviation stem:[sigma] of this distribution are estimated
from the sample statistics.

NOTE: A process for which mean values and standard deviations show acceptable
variation with respect to specified limits, from sample to sample, is known
as a stable process.

[[sec_B.1.4]]
==== {blank}

The prior PDF stem:[g_0 (eta)] for a measurand stem:[Y] then takes the
mathematical form of the frequency distribution suggested by a histogram
of measured quantity values. The property of interest for an item chosen
at random from the process would then be assigned a best estimate
stem:[y_0 = mu] and associated standard uncertainty stem:[u_0 = sigma].

[[sec_B.1.5]]
==== {blank}

This typical SPC procedure has two principal shortcomings: (a) reliable
process modelling by use of a histogram usually requires a large sample,
which might not be available, and (b) the uncertainty associated with the
sample measurements is ignored and plays no role in assigning the PDF
stem:[g_0 (eta)]. The following approach addresses both of these problems
and reduces to the SPC result in the appropriate limits.

[[sec_B.2]]
=== An item chosen at random from a measured sample of items

[[sec_B.2.1]]
==== {blank}

Consider a sample of stem:[n] items, each having a property stem:[Y] of
interest in a conformity assessment. The sample is collected over an appropriate
period of time from a production process that is assumed to be stable.
Examples include:

* a sample of stem:[n] gauge blocks, each characterized by a length stem:[L];

* a sample of stem:[n] digital voltmeters, each characterized by an error
of indication stem:[E] when measuring a standard reference voltage;

* a sample of stem:[n] fibre optic connectors, each characterized by an
insertion loss stem:[Lambda].

[[sec_B.2.2]]
==== {blank}

For each of the stem:[n] items the property of interest is measured, yielding
a set of estimates stem:[y_1, ... , y_n] and an associated standard measurement
uncertainty eu: The uncertainty eu depends on the procedure used to measure
the samples and is assumed to be the same for all of the measurements.
The properties of the sample are then summarized by calculating a sample
mean stem:[y] and sample variance stem:[s^2] defined by

[[eq_B.1]]
[stem]
++++
bar y = 1/n sum_{k=1}^n y_k,
++++

and

[[eq_B.2]]
[stem]
++++
s^2 = 1/n sum_{k=1}^n (y_k - bar(y))^2 .
++++

NOTE: The sample variance is often defined by dividing the
stem:[sum_{k=1}^n (y_k - y)^2] by stem:[n - 1] rather than stem:[n]. The
resulting quantity can be shown to be an unbiased estimator of the variance
stem:[sigma^2] of a frequency distribution from which the data samples
are assumed to be drawn. In defining stem:[s^2] in <<eq_B.2>>, the goal
is not to estimate an unknown variance but rather to characterise the dispersion
of the sample values about their mean. If the data are assumed to be a
random sample from a normal frequency distribution
stem:[varphi (eta, mu; sigma^2)], then the sample variance (<<eq_B.2>>)
can be shown to be the maximum likelihood estimator of stem:[sigma^2] <<ref10>>.

[[sec_B.2.3]]
==== {blank}

One of the measured items is selected at random (with probability stem:[1//n])
and taken to be representative of the production process. Let stem:[Y_r]
denote the property of interest for the randomly-selected item. Information
relevant to the possible values eta of stem:[Y_r] consists only of the
sample statistics (<<eq_B.1>>) and (<<eq_B.2>>), the individual estimates
stem:[y_1, ..., y_n] being discarded once the measurements have been performed.
Summary properties of the PDF for stem:[Y_r] can be calculated as follows.

[[sec_B.2.4]]
==== {blank}

Let stem:[f_r(eta)] be the PDF for stem:[Y_r] and denote the PDFs for the
stem:[n] sample properties stem:[Y_1, ..., Y_n] by
stem:[f_k(eta), k = 1, ..., n]. Noting that each of the n items is equally
likely to have been selected, stem:[f_r(eta)] can be written as the marginal
PDF

[[eq_B.3]]
[stem]
++++
f_r(eta) = 1/n sum_{k=1}^n f_k (eta),
++++

called, appropriately, a finite mixture distribution <<ref41>>.

[[sec_B.2.5]]
==== {blank}

The form of a particular PDF stem:[f_k(eta)] is generally not known, but
since it conveys knowledge of the property stem:[Y_k] of the stem:[k"th"]
measured item,

[[eq_B.4]]
[stem]
++++
E(Y_k) = y_k =  int_{-oo}^{oo} eta f_k (eta) "d"eta ,
++++

and

[[eq_B.5]]
[stem]
++++
V(Y_k) = tilde(u)^2 = int_{-oo}^{oo} (eta - y_k)^2 f_k (eta) "d"eta .
++++

Given these results and the PDF stem:[f_r(eta)] of <<eq_B.3>>, the estimate
stem:[y_r] of the property stem:[Y_r] and associated standard uncertainty
stem:[u_r] can then be calculated.

[[sec_B.2.6]]
==== {blank}

For the estimate stem:[y_r] we have, by definition

[stem%unnumbered]
++++
y_r = int_{-oo}^{oo} eta f_r (eta) "d"eta = 1/n sum_{k=1}^n int_{-oo}^{oo} eta f_k (eta) "d"eta = 1/n sum_{k=1}^n y_k ,
++++

where <<eq_B.4>> has been used in the last step. Comparing this result
with <<eq_B.1>> shows that the a _priori_ estimate of stem:[Y] is equal
to the sample mean:

[[eq_B.6]]
[stem]
++++
y_r = bar(y) .
++++

[[sec_B.2.7]]
==== {blank}

The associated variance of stem:[Y], whose positive square root is the
standard uncertainty, is then given by

[[eq_B.7]]
[stem]
++++
u_r^2 = int_{-oo}^{oo} (eta - bar(y))^2 f_r(eta) "d"eta = 1/n sum_{k=1}^n int_{-oo}^{oo} (eta - bar(y))^2 f_k(eta) "d"eta .
++++

Now writing

[stem]
++++
(eta - bar(y))^2 = (eta - y_k + y_k - bar(y))^2 = (eta - y_k)^2 + (y_k - bar(y))^2 + 2(eta - y_k)(y_k - bar(y)) ,
++++

using <<eq_B.4>> and <<eq_B.5>>, and substituting into <<eq_B.7>> leads
to the result

[[eq_B.8]]
[stem]
++++
u_r^2 = tilde(u)^2 + 1/n sum_{k=1}^n (y_k - bar(y))^2 .
++++

[[sec_B.2.8]]
==== {blank}

The sum on the right-hand side of <<eq_B.8>> is seen to be the sample variance
stem:[s^2] [see <<eq_B.2>>] so that

[[eq_B.9]]
[stem]
++++
u_r^2 = tilde(u)^2 + s^2 ,
++++

and the standard uncertainty associated with the prior estimate
stem:[y_0] is

[[eq_B.10]]
[stem]
++++
u_r = sqrt(tilde(u)^2 + s^2) .
++++

[[sec_B.2.9]]
==== {blank}

The standard uncertainty stem:[u_r] given by <<eq_B.10>> is seen to be
a quadrature [or root-sum-square (RSS)] combination of two components which
are just the two parameters that summarize the sample data: a term stem:[tilde(u)^2]
due to the common standard uncertainty associated with the sample measurements,
and a term stem:[s^2] that characterizes the variability of the estimates
stem:[y_1, ..., y_n].

NOTE: Variability due to process variation and lack of measurement repeatability
are combined in the observed sample variance stem:[s^2]. The standard uncertainty
stem:[tilde(u)] should include a component that captures the effect of
measurement variation.

[[sec_B.2.10]]
==== {blank}

The calculated estimate and sample variance for the randomly selected item,
<<eq_B.6>> and <<eq_B.7>>, are then taken to characterize future production
of the process, assumed to be stable and free of drift. The logical model
has a metrologist or inspector reasoning as follows:

____
I have chosen a future item from the production process. What can I say
about the property stem:[Y] of this item before it is measured? Based on
the results of the sample measurements, I believe that the best estimate
of stem:[Y] is stem:[y_0 = y_r], given by <<eq_B.6>>, with an associated
variance stem:[u_0^2 = u_r^2] given by <<eq_B.9>>. This is the extent of
my knowledge. Given this information and the principle of maximum entropy
(see JCGM 101:2008 6.3 and reference <<ref45>>), I will assign a normal
PDF to convey and encode my prior knowledge of the property stem:[Y] for
this item.
____

[[sec_B.2.11]]
==== {blank}

This leads to the following normal (or Gaussian) distribution to encode
prior knowledge of property stem:[Y] :

[stem]
++++
g_0 (eta) = 1/{u_0 sqrt(2pi)} exp [-1/2 ({eta - y_0}/{u_0})^2] = varphi (eta; y_0, u_0^2) ,
++++

with stem:[y_0 = bar(y)] and stem:[u_0^2 = tilde(u)^2 + s^2].

[[sec_B.2.12]]
==== {blank}

In the common case where stem:[s^2 " >> " tilde(u)^2], uncertainty in the
value of a property of an item chosen at random from the production process
is dominated by process variability. Then stem:[u_0 ~~ sigma ~~ s], where
the process is modelled by a frequency distribution with a standard deviation
stem:[sigma] estimated by the sample standard deviation stem:[s].

[[sec_B.3]]
=== A positive property near a physical limit

[[sec_B.3.1]]
==== {blank}

The normal PDF has an infinite range. In the case of a property (measurand)
that is strictly positive, an assigned normal PDF will distribute a fraction
of its probability over negative (and thus impossible) values of the property.
For a property whose best estimate is within a few times its associated
standard uncertainty of zero, this fraction of the probability can be significant.
In such a case, assigning a normal PDF would be an unreasonable way to
encode knowledge of the measurand.

[[sec_B.3.2]]
==== {blank}

Many well-known PDFs are restricted to positive values of their arguments.
Depending on available information, such a PDF can serve to model knowledge
of a measurand stem:[Y] near a physical limit. In the case where knowledge
of stem:[Y >= 0] is limited to an estimate and associated variance, as
in clause <<sec_B.2>>, the principle of maximum entropy leads to the assignment
of a normal distribution that is truncated at zero <<ref11>>. If values
of stem:[Y] near zero are believed to have negligible probability, assigning
a prior PDF stem:[g_0 (eta)] that approaches zero as stem:[eta -> 0] might
be appropriate. One such distribution is the gamma PDF, whose use will
serve as an example.

[[sec_B.3.3]]
==== {blank}

The gamma PDF, with positive parameters stem:[alpha] and stem:[lambda],
is defined by

[[eq_B.11]]
[stem]
++++
"gamma"(eta; alpha, lambda) = {lambda^{alpha}}/{Gamma(alpha)} eta^{alpha - 1} e^{-alpha eta} , " " eta >= 0
++++

where -(ff) is the gamma function:

[stem%unnumbered]
++++
Gamma(alpha) = int_0^{oo} x^{alpha - 1} e^{-x} "d"x .
++++

NOTE: Special cases of the gamma PDF include stem:["gamma" (eta; 1; lambda)]
(an exponential PDF with parameter lambda) and
stem:["gamma" (eta; n//2, 1//2)] (a chi-squared PDF with stem:[n] degrees
of freedom).

NOTE: It is possible to define a 3-parameter gamma distribution by replacing
stem:[eta] with stem:[(eta - gamma)] in <<eq_B.11>>, where the parameter
 becomes the left end-point and the distribution is restricted to the interval
stem:[eta >= gamma].

[[sec_B.3.4]]
==== {blank}

<<fig_B.1>> shows four gamma PDFs for particular values of stem:[alpha]
and stem:[lambda]. The expectation and variance of the gamma PDF are
given by

[[eq_B.12]]
[stem]
++++
E(Y ) = y_0 = alpha/lambda, " " V (Y) = u_0^2 = {alpha}/{lambda^2} ,
++++

and the maximum value (mode) of the distribution occurs when

[[eq_B.13]]
[stem]
++++
eta = {alpha - 1}/{lambda} .
++++

[[sec_B.3.5]]
==== {blank}

Given a particular state of prior information, appropriate values of stem:[alpha]
and stem:[lambda] can be estimated using these expressions. In the case
where knowledge of a property (measurand) stem:[Y] is obtained by measuring
a sample of produced items, the prior estimate and associated variance
are estimated by the sample statistics: stem:[y_0 = bar(y)] and
stem:[u_0^2 = s^2]. <<eq_B.12>> can then be solved for the gamma PDF parameters:

[[eq_B.14]]
[stem]
++++
alpha = {bar(y)^2}/{s^2}, " " lambda = {bar(y)}/{s^2} .
++++

These estimates are the so-called 'method of moments' estimates, and may
be unsatisfactory for a small sample size. Alternatives are the maximum
likelihood estimates, but these typically require some form of numerical
optimization or the solution of a system of non-linear equations.

[[sec_B.3.6]]
==== {blank}

An example using a gamma PDF in the calculation of consumer's and producer's
risks is given in clause <<sec_9.5.4>>.
