[[cls-7]]
== Modelling the measurement principle

[[cls-7.1]]
=== General

The measurement principle (see <<jcgm200,clause=2.4>>) enables a basic model, often
based on a scientific law, or combination of such laws, to be established. A set of
input quantities, namely, those quantities appearing in the laws underlying the
measurement, follows from the measurement principle.

NOTE: In many standardized test methods, as published by ISO and IEC for instance,
the measurement principle is already described in the form of one or more
mathematical formulæ.

[[ex-7.1]]
[example]
.Simple theoretical model of mass measurement
====
The mass stem:[m] of a weight is measured with a spring balance. The relationship
between the restoring force stem:[F] exerted by the spring and stem:[m] at
equilibrium is stem:[F = mg], where stem:[g] is the acceleration due to gravity.
Hooke's law relating stem:[F] to the extension stem:[X] of the spring and the spring
constant stem:[k] is stem:[F = kX]. Thus, a theoretical measurement model relating
mass to extension, spring constant and acceleration due to gravity is

[stem%unnumbered]
++++
m = k/g X.
++++

Available knowledge about stem:[X], stem:[k] and stem:[g] enables knowledge about
stem:[m] to be inferred. Since Hooke's law only applies when the extension is
sufficiently small, this measurement model should only be used within this limit. The
largest mass, for a given stem:[k], for which the restoring force is proportional to
the extension of the spring defines the domain of validity of the measurement model
(see <<cls-5.8>>).
====

=== Theoretical, empirical and hybrid measurement models

[[cls-7.2.1]]
==== {blank}

A theoretical measurement model is based on scientific theory that describes how the
output quantities in the measurement model relate to the input quantities.

[[ex1-7.2.1]]
[example]
.SI value of the Avogadro constant
====
The Avogadro constant stem:[N_"A"] is the universal proportionality constant relating
in any sample the number stem:[N("X")] of entities stem:["X"] and the corresponding
amount of substance stem:[n("X")],

[stem%unnumbered]
++++
N_"A" = (N("X"))/(n("X")).
++++

In the SI, the Avogadro constant has the exact value stem:[N_"A" = 6.02214076 xx
10^(23) "unitsml(mol^(-1))"] <<newell>>. One of the most accurate methods that was used to
determine this value is the X-ray crystal density (XRCD) method <<kuramoto>>. In XRCD, the
sample is a near-perfect sphere of mono-crystalline, isotopically enriched silicon
(99.99&nbsp;% stem:[""^(28)"Si"]). The number of silicon atoms in this sample is
stem:[N("Si") = 8 V//a_0^3], stem:[V] and stem:[a_0^3] being respectively the volumes
of the sphere and of the unit atomic cell, containing stem:[8] atoms. The amount of
silicon is stem:[n("Si") = m//M], where stem:[m] and stem:[M] are the mass of the
sphere and the molar mass of silicon. Therefore the definition of the Avogadro
constant may be written as

[stem%unnumbered]
++++
N_"A" = (N("Si"))/(n("Si")) = (8V)/a_0^3 M/m,
++++

which is the basic model describing the measurement principle for the XRCD experiment.
====

[[ex2-7.2.1]]
[example]
.Pressure generated by a pressure balance (continued in <<cls-8.1.4>>)
====
The pressure stem:[p] generated by a pressure balance is given implicitly by the
measurement model

[[eq3]]
[stem]
++++
p = (m(1-rho_a/rho_m)g)/(A_0(1+lambda p)[1+alpha(t-t_"ref")]),
++++

where stem:[m] is the total applied mass, stem:[rho_a] and stem:[rho_m] are
respectively the mass densities of air and the applied mass, stem:[g] is the local
acceleration due to gravity, stem:[A_0] is the effective area of the piston-cylinder
assembly at zero pressure and a reference temperature, stem:[lambda] is the
distortion coefficient of the piston-cylinder assembly, stem:[alpha] is the
temperature coefficient, stem:[t] is Celsius temperature, and stem:[t_"ref"] is a
reference temperature, stem:[20 "unitsml(degC)"], say <<lewis>>. There are eight input
quantities stem:[A_0], stem:[lambda], stem:[alpha], stem:[t], stem:[m], stem:[rho_a],
stem:[rho_m], stem:[g] and a single output quantity stem:[p].
====

[[cls-7.2.2]]
==== {blank}

Sometimes the measurement principle can only be formulated in terms of an empirical
model. For such a model, the measurand would often be expressed in terms of
mathematical functions such as polynomials, polynomial splines or rational functions,
all of which have adjustable parameters. These models are often expressed as
statistical models (see <<cls-11>>). Estimates of the parameters of these functions
can be provided by reference tables (for instance, see <<ex2-8.3.3>>)
or obtained by data analysis (least-squares regression, say), together
with the associated standard uncertainties and covariances.(((covariance)))
(((polynomial spline)))

[[ex-7.2.1]]
[example]
.Industrial platinum resistance thermometer
====
Platinum resistance thermometers of nominal resistance stem:[100 "unitsml(Ohm)"],
also called Pt100 sensors, are used in industry for accurate measurement of
temperature. The resistance stem:[R] of the sensor as a function of Celsius
temperature stem:[t] is modelled over the interval stem:[[-200 "unitsml(degC)", 800
"unitsml(degC)"\]] using the empirical Callendar-Van Dusen equation <<dusen>>

[[eq4]]
[stem]
++++
R(t) = {(R_0[1+At+Bt^2+Ct^3(t-t_"ref")]", ",-200 "unitsml(degC)" <= t < 0 "unitsml(degC)"","),(R_0(1+At+Bt^2)", ", 0 "unitsml(degC)" <= t <= 800 "unitsml(degC)"","):}
++++

where stem:[t_"ref" = 100 "unitsml(degC)"], being a reference value, has no
associated uncertainty, and stem:[R_0], stem:[A], stem:[B] and stem:[C] are
determined by calibration. Typical values are stem:[hat R_0= 100 "unitsml(Ohm)"],
stem:[hat A = 3.9 xx 10^(-3) "unitsml(degC^(-1))"], stem:[hat B = -6 xx 10^(-7)
"unitsml(degC^(-2))"], and stem:[hat C = -4 xx 10^(-12) "unitsml(degC^(-4))"].

<<eq4>> is then used to provide values of the measurand stem:[t] given values for the
input quantities stem:[R_0], stem:[A], stem:[B], stem:[C] and stem:[R] and is thus an
implicit measurement model (see <<cls-13.5>>).(((measurement model,implicit)))
====

[[cls-7.2.3]]
==== {blank}

Most measurement models are hybrid, that is, they combine aspects of theoretical and
empirical models. Even if the complete measurement principle is based on scientific
theory, there are often quantities involved in the measurement that need to be
included in the measurement model and are modelled empirically (also see
<<cls-9;and!cls-10>>).

[[ex-7.2.3]]
[example]
.Spectral irradiance of a lamp: hybrid model
====
A tungsten filament lamp source emits light when its filament is heated by an
electric current. The primary physics behind this process is that of a hot source and
to first order the lamp acts in a manner similar to a blackbody source. The spectral
radiance of a blackbody source can be modelled using the Planck function

[stem%unnumbered]
++++
L(lambda,T) = (c_(1,L))/(lambda^5{"exp"[c_2//(lambda T)]-1}),
++++

where stem:[lambda] denotes wavelength, stem:[T] the blackbody temperature and the
coefficients stem:[c_(1,L)] and stem:[c_2] can be expressed in terms of fundamental
physical constants <<andor>>.

Since lamps are not ideal blackbody sources, the emissivity of the tungsten filament
and the transmittance of the bulb should be considered. For tungsten filament lamps
used as standards, their spectral irradiance varies smoothly with wavelength and can
be modelled by the product of a polynomial in stem:[lambda] and a Planck function.
(Spectral irradiance is related to radiance by a constant factor depending on
geometry.) Such a function is known as a Planck-((polynomial function)) <<cox41>>, <<huang>>:

[[eq5]]
[stem]
++++
F(lambda,T,a_1, ..., a_p) = L(lambda,T)G_p(lambda,a_1,...,a_p).
++++

The correction factor stem:[G_p(lambda, a_1, ..., a_p)] is an empirical polynomial
function of order stem:[p] in stem:[lambda] with adjustable parameters stem:[a_1,
..., a_p]. These parameters are estimated by fitting <<eq5>> to suitable data using
regression analysis.
====

[[cls-7.3]]
=== Differential equation models

[[cls-7.3.1]]
==== {blank}

Many theoretical models used in science, engineering and medicine specify the rates
at which some quantities vary as functions of time and other quantities, and are
formulated as differential equations <<simmons>>. In some cases, the differential equation
is only used as a foundation to develop simpler models.

[[ex-7.3.1]]
[example]
.Vapour pressure at vapour-liquid equilibrium
====
The vapour pressure equations used in <<ex1-8.1.4>> are solutions of
the Clausius-Clapeyron relation, which is a differential equation,

[[eq6]]
[stem]
++++
("d"p)/("d"T) = (Delta s)/(Delta nu),
++++

where stem:[p] denotes the pressure, the measurand, and stem:[T] the thermodynamic
temperature of the vapour-liquid equilibrium, and stem:[Delta s] and stem:[Delta v]
the differences in specific entropy and molar volume, respectively, between the two
phases <<atkins>>.
====

[[cls-7.3.2]]
==== {blank}

In measurement models that involve differential equations, the measurand is typically
a function of coefficients in these equations. These coefficients are estimated based
on empirical data, usually by application of a statistical method (see also
<<cls-11>>). The equations are solved in the process, often a number of times,
usually employing numerical methods, and typically a ((Monte Carlo method)), similar to
that described in <<jcgm101>>, is used for uncertainty evaluation
<<chkrebtii>>, <<eichstadt55>>, <<possolo137>>, <<soetaert>>.(((measurement model,involving differential equations)))

[[ex-7.3.2]]
[example]
.SIR model for influenza infection
====
The numbers of cases reported once per week during an influenza epidemic in an
isolated village with stem:[n = 327] inhabitants, during the first 11 weeks of their
influenza season were (1, 0), (8, 0), (15, 4), (22, 26), (29, 61), (36, 67), (43,
42), (50, 40), (57, 23), (64, 13), (71, 5). The first element of each pair is the day
since the beginning of the season, and the second is the number of people known to
have influenza on that day. For instance, the first pair means that on day 1 there
were 0 cases, and the last pair means that on day 71 there were 5 cases.

The simple SIR model <<kermack>> for the spread of infectious diseases through a population
does not contemplate births or deaths occurring during the course of the epidemic. It
regards the population as partitioned into three subsets at each time instant
stem:[t]: those that are susceptible to the infection but not yet sick, stem:[S(t)],
those that are infected, stem:[I(t)], and those that have already recovered from the
infection and are no longer contagious, stem:[R(t)]. Their numbers add up to the
population total, stem:[S(t) + I (t) + R(t) = n], and satisfy three simultaneous
differential equations:

[[eq7]]
[stem]
++++
{:(("d"S)/("d"t)=-beta I(t) S(t)//n", ", ("d"I)/("d"t) = beta I(t)S(t)//n - gamma I(t)", ", ("d"R)/("d"t)=gamma I(t)","):}
++++

for some specified non-negative initial values stem:[S(0)], stem:[I(0)] and
stem:[R(0)]. The model parameters are such that stem:[1//gamma] is the typical
duration of the infection in an infected person and the parameter of interest is the
the average number of individuals directly infected by an infectious person in a
population where everyone is susceptible, known as the _basic reproduction number_
stem:[R_0 = beta//gamma]. The model is completed by assuming that the actual number
of cases at time stem:[t] is a random variable stem:[N(t)] with a Poisson
distribution with mean stem:[I(t)].
(((probability distribution,Poisson)))

Computing the maximum-likelihood estimates <<pawitan>> of stem:[beta], stem:[gamma] and
stem:[S(0)] involves finding the values of these parameters that maximize a product
of Poisson probability densities (one for each of the 11 days with a reported number
of cases), whose means are stem:[I(1), I(8), ..., I(71)]. Since <<eq7>> cannot be
solved analytically, each time the likelihood function needs to be evaluated in the
course of the maximization process, these differential equations have to be solved,
also numerically. The resulting parameter estimates are stem:[hat beta = 0.34 "
d"^(-1)] and stem:[hat gamma = 0.14 " d"^(-1)], and hence stem:[hat R_0 = 2.4].
<<fig2>> (left) depicts the data, the model as fitted to the observations, and the
fitted values on the same days when the numbers of influenza cases were observed.
(((maximum-likelihood method)))

[[fig2]]
.Observed numbers of cases (large, red dots), calibrated stem:[hat I] (solid blue curve), and expected numbers of cases (small blue dots) corresponding to the observed numbers of cases and (right) probability density of stem:[R_0] based on a sample of size 1 000 drawn from its distribution by a Monte Carlo method, with the blue dot marking the maximum-likelihood estimate, the shaded area comprising 95&nbsp;% of the area under the curve, and the thick, horizontal red line segment representing a 95&nbsp;% coverage interval for the reproduction number
image::img02.png[]

The uncertainty associated with the basic reproduction number was evaluated by
application of the parametric bootstrap <<efron>>, a ((Monte Carlo method)). This method is
particularly onerous computationally, because it involves repeating the following
steps a substantial number stem:[K] of times: (i) draw one sample value from each of
the 11 Poisson distributions whose means are the values stem:[hat I(1), hat I(8),
..., hat I(71)] corresponding to the solution of <<eq7>> for stem:[beta = hat beta],
stem:[gamma = hat gamma] and stem:[S_0 = hat S_0]; (ii) estimate the same parameters,
again via maximum likelihood, using the simulated draws, and compute the
corresponding value of the basic reproduction number.

A sample of modest size stem:[K = 1000] drawn from the probability distribution of
stem:[R_0] as just described (<<fig2>>, right) had standard deviation stem:[0.12],
which is used as the standard uncertainty stem:[u(R0)] associated with stem:[R_0]. A
95&nbsp;% coverage interval for R0 deduced from the sample ranged from stem:[2.2] to
stem:[2.7].
====
