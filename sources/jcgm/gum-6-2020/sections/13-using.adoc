[[cls-13]]
== Using the measurement model

[[cls-13.1]]
=== General

[[cls-13.1.1]]
==== {blank}

The main use of a measurement model is to calculate an estimate of the measurand and
the associated uncertainty. Depending on the requirements, the uncertainty can be
provided as a standard uncertainty, a coverage interval for a stipulated coverage
probability, or both. Especially when using Monte Carlo or ((Bayesian methods)), it can
be more appropriate to provide a (representation of) the probability distribution for
the measurand. The latter is especially true when the output of the measurement model
is used in a subsequent uncertainty calculation taking a probability distribution as
input.

[[cls-13.1.2]]
==== {blank}

Given values for the input quantities in the measurement model, the corresponding
value of the measurand is obtained by evaluating a formula or by solving an equation.
Some quantities can take complex values. There can be a single (scalar) measurand or
multiple measurands (vector measurand). The mathematical, statistical or numerical
tools applied for obtaining the estimate of the measurand and uncertainty evaluation
depend on the form of the measurement model.

[[cls-13.1.3]]
==== {blank}

The law of propagation of uncertainty holds exactly for a linear measurement model,
that is, a model that is linear in its input quantities, but not generally otherwise.
For a single output quantity, a linear measurement model takes the form

[[eq27]]
[stem]
++++
Y = c_0 + c_1X_1 + ... + c_N X_N,
++++

where stem:[c_0, ...,c_N] are exactly known constants. This expression is
straightforwardly generalized to multivariate models, and to those involving complex
quantities. Guidance on how to use the law of propagation of uncertainty is given in
<<jcgm100>> for explicit univariate measurement models [as in <<eq1>>] and in
<<jcgm102>> for other forms, including multivariate measurement models.
(((measurement model,univariate,explicit)))

[[cls-13.1.4]]
==== {blank}

A non-linear measurement model is a model that cannot exactly be expressed in the
form <<eq27>>. With some degree of approximation, a non-linear model can be expressed
locally in linear form in the neighbourhood of estimates stem:[x_1, ..., x_N] of the
input quantities. On the basis of this linear form the law of propagation of
uncertainty can be applied to yield an approximation to the standard uncertainty
associated with the estimate stem:[y = f(x_1, ..., x_N)] of the output quantity. The
adequacy of this approximation can be checked in any particular instance by applying
the ((Monte Carlo method)) of <<jcgm101,clause=7>>, or <<jcgm102,clause=7>> in a
multivariate case. <<annexF>> gives further details of model linearization and provides
a simple method (that is not foolproof) of checking the approximation.
(((measurement model,non-linear)))(((measurement model,linearization)))

[[ex-13.1.4]]
[example]
.Failure of the law of propagation of uncertainty to propagate uncertainty in a non-linear model
====
Bromine has two stable isotopes, bromine-79 and bromine-81. Consider a dibromine
molecule (stem:["Br"_2]) whose most abundant molecule is stem:[""^(79)"Br"^(81)"Br"]
(the other molecules being stem:[""^(79)"Br"^(79)"Br"] and
stem:[""^(81)"Br"^(81)"Br"]). The abundance of this molecule, having a mass of
stem:[160 " Da"], with respect to all molecules of stem:["Br"_2] is given by the
expression <<meija120>>

[stem%unnumbered]
++++
x_(160) = 2x_(79)(1 - x_(79)).
++++

The isotopic abundance stem:[x_(79)] of bromine-79 varies naturally from stem:[0.505]
to stem:[0.508]. Consider that stem:["Br"_2] has been made from a slightly altered
isotopic bromine with stem:[x_(79) = 0.500] and stem:[u(x_(79)) = 0.005], and the
abundance stem:[x_(160)] of stem:[""^(79)"Br"^(81)"Br"] is required. In such a case,
the application of the law of propagation of uncertainty for the above measurement
model yields

[stem%unnumbered]
++++
u^2(x_(160)) = (("d"x_(160))/("d"x_(79)))^2 u^2(x_(79)) = (2-4x_(79))^2 u^2 (x_(79)).
++++

In this instance, the law of propagation of uncertainty provides invalid results
since it yields stem:[u(x_(160)) = 0] when stem:[x_(79) = 0.500], regardless of the
uncertainty associated with stem:[x_(79)]. The inadequate linearization of this model
as used by the law of propagation of uncertainty can be remedied by using the Monte
Carlo method: by modelling stem:[x_(79)] by a normal random variable,
stem:["N"(0.500, 0.005^2)], for instance, stem:[x_(160) = 0.49995] with
stem:[u(x_(180)) = 0.00007] is obtained.

The issue can also be overcome by calculating non-symmetric approximations for the
sensitivity coefficients or by including higher-order terms <<meija120>>.
====

[[cls-13.2]]
=== Use of a model beyond the range for which it has been validated

The use of a measurement model for ((extrapolation)) beyond an interval over which it is
deemed valid is generally inadvisable (also see <<cls-5.8>> and <<ex-10.6.4>>). It is
generally unsafe to fit an empirical or hybrid model to data and use the model beyond
the span of the fitted data. Extrapolation with purely empirical functions such as
polynomials and polynomial splines is particularly dangerous. Such functions can
exhibit spurious behaviour beyond the span of the data they explain and should not be
used for this purpose. In certain circumstances, a theoretical model may be used with
caution for extrapolation beyond the span of the data that defines it when the model
is judged to describe adequately the phenomenon of interest.(((polynomial spline)))

[[ex-13.2]]
[example]
.Isotope-based quantitation: use of theoretical and empirical models
====
In isotope-based quantitation, routinely employed in chemical measurement, the
measurand is the isotope amount ratio of a substance of interest (the analyte) in an
'unknown' sample. This ratio is specified by a calibration function giving the
relationship between isotope amount ratio and mass ratio. The calibration function is
provided by regression given a data set consisting of several pairs of values. Each
pair corresponds to a 'known' sample, for which a value of stem:[R_"AB"], the isotope
ratio of the mixture, is observed (measured) corresponding to a value of stem:[q],
the mass ratio of the sample (analyte) stem:["A"] and the isotopically-labelled
internal standard stem:["B"]. See the data points indicated by filled circles in
<<fig9>>.

[[fig9]]
.Isotope dilution theoretical (rational) model and empirical models fitted to calibration data and used for prediction
image::img09.png[]

A feature that distinguishes isotope dilution from many other methods of analytical
chemistry is the inherently non-linear theoretical calibration function,

[[eq28]]
[stem]
++++
R_"AB" = (a_0 + a_1q)/(1+a_2q),
++++

which is a rational function having polynomial degree 1 in the numerator and in the
denominator, where stem:[a_0], stem:[a_1] and stem:[a_2] are parameters calculated by
regression from the provided data. Then, for a sample having a specified value of
mass ratio stem:[q], the corresponding value of stem:[R_"AB"] is determined from
the <<eq28>>. Accordingly, the calibration function given by <<eq28>> is an explicit
univariate model (see <<cls-13.3>>) in which stem:[R_"AB"] is the output quantity or
measurand, and the input quantities are stem:[q] and the calibration parameters
stem:[a_0], stem:[a_1] and stem:[a_2].

Many current implementations of isotope dilution calibration either ignore the
curvature of the function (due to the presence of the term involving stem:[a_2]) or
use instead an empirical model such as a polynomial of degree 2 or 3 (see
<<annexD>>). Such empirical models do not generally extrapolate well beyond the
interval spanned by the calibration data. <<fig9>> shows the use of a linear
polynomial or straight line (corresponding to ignoring the term stem:[a_2q]), and
quadratic and cubic polynomials fitted to five calibration points (filled circles).
It also shows the <<eq28>> fitted to the same data. The open circles correspond to two
additional samples outside the span of the calibration data. The advantage of the
theoretical model over the candidate empirical models is evident in this case.
Further details are given in reference <<pagliano>>.
====

[[cls-13.3]]
=== Explicit univariate measurement model

A measurement model taking the form <<eq1>> relating a single output quantity
stem:[Y] to the input quantities stem:[X_1, ..., X_N] is termed univariate and
explicit. Given values for the input quantities stem:[X_1, ..., X_N], an explicit
model enables the corresponding value of the output quantity stem:[Y] to be
determined directly. This form of model is the simplest and can be handled by the
provisions of <<jcgm100>> or <<jcgm101>>.

[[ex-13.3]]
[example]
.Gauge-block measurement (also see <<ex-6.2>> and <<ex1-6.5>>)
====
The length of a nominally stem:[50 "unitsml(mm)"] gauge block is determined by
comparing it with a known gauge-block measurement standard of the same nominal length
using a gauge block comparator. An expression for the direct output of the comparison
of the two gauge blocks is the difference

[[eq29]]
[stem]
++++
d = [1+(alpha_"S" + delta alpha) theta] &#120001; - [1+alpha_"S"(theta - delta theta)] &#120001;_"S"
++++

in their lengths, where

* stem:[&#120001;] is the length at stem:[20 "unitsml(degC)"] of the gauge block
being calibrated,
* stem:[&#120001;_"S"] is the length of the gauge-block measurement standard at
stem:[20 "unitsml(degC)"] as given on its calibration certificate,
* stem:[alpha_"S"] is the coefficient of thermal expansion of the gauge-block
measurement standard,
* stem:[delta alpha = alpha - alpha_"S"], where stem:[alpha] is the coefficient of
thermal expansion of the gauge block being calibrated,
* stem:[theta] is the deviation in Celsius temperature from the stem:[20
"unitsml(degC)"] reference temperature of the gauge block being calibrated, and
* stem:[delta theta = theta - theta_"S"], where stem:[theta_"S"] is the deviation in
Celsius temperature from the stem:[20 "unitsml(degC)"] reference temperature of the
gauge-block measurement standard.

From <<eq29>> the output quantity stem:[&#120001;] can be expressed in terms of the
stem:[N = 6] input quantities stem:[d], stem:[&#120001;_"S"], stem:[alpha_"S"],
stem:[delta alpha], stem:[theta] and stem:[delta theta] as the explicit univariate
measurement model

[[eq30]]
[stem]
++++
&#120001; = ([1+alpha_"S"(theta - delta theta)]&#120001;_"S" + d)/(1+(alpha_"S"+delta alpha)theta),
++++

the right-hand side of <<eq30>> constituting the measurement function.
====

[[cls-13.4]]
=== Explicit multivariate measurement model

A measurement model taking the form

[[eq31]]
[stem]
++++
{: (Y_1 = f_1(X_1, ..., X_N)", ", ...", ", Y_m=f_m(X_1, ..., X_N)".") :}
++++

where stem:[Y_1, ..., Y_m] are stem:[m] output quantities, and stem:[f_1, ..., f_m]
denote the stem:[m] measurement functions, is termed multivariate and explicit. This
form of model can be handled by the provisions of <<jcgm102>>.

[[ex-13.4]]
[example]
.Resistance and reactance of a circuit element
====
The resistance stem:[R] and reactance stem:[X] of a circuit element are given by
<<jcgm100,example=H.2>>:

[[eq32]]
[stem]
++++
{: (R=V/i "cos "phi", ", X = V/I "sin "phi".") :}
++++

Here <<eq32>> is the explicit multivariate measurement model for resistance and
reactance in which stem:[V] denotes potential difference, stem:[I] denotes current
and stem:[phi] denotes phase angle. There are stem:[N = 3] input quantities,
stem:[V], stem:[I] and stem:[phi], and stem:[m = 2] output quantities stem:[R] and
stem:[X]. The symbol stem:[X], conventionally denoting reactance, is not to be
confused with stem:[X_1, X_2], etc., the generic symbols for the input quantities.
====

[[cls-13.5]]
=== Implicit univariate measurement model

A measurement model taking the form

[[eq33]]
[stem]
++++
h(Y, X_1, ..., X_N) = 0,
++++

where stem:[Y] is a scalar output quantity, and stem:[h] denotes a function of
stem:[Y] and the input quantities stem:[X_1, ..., X_N], is termed univariate and
implicit. Given values for the input quantities stem:[X_1, ..., X_N], the
corresponding value of the output quantity stem:[Y] cannot be calculated directly
from an implicit model. Specifically, <<eq33>> cannot readily or numerically stably
be represented in terms of a measurement function. It is generally solved
numerically, often by using an iterative procedure, to obtain a value of stem:[Y]
corresponding to given values of stem:[X_1, ..., X_N]. This form of model can be
handled by the provisions of <<jcgm102>>, which covers a generalization of the
univariate form of the law of propagation of uncertainty given in
<<jcgm100,clause=5.1;and!clause=5.2>> and a ((Monte Carlo method)).
(((measurement model,implicit)))

NOTE: There is not a unique way to write an implicit measurement model. For instance,
in place of <<eq3>> in <<ex2-7.2.1>> the measurement model given by equating to zero
the difference between the left- and right-hand sides of <<eq3>> could be used, which
will then take the form <<eq33>>. The numerical efficiency of the solution of the
measurement model equation depends on the choice made.

NOTE: It is assumed that the implicit univariate measurement model (<<eq33>>) can be
solved uniquely for the value of stem:[Y] corresponding to given values of stem:[X_1,
..., X_N] close to their estimates.
(((measurement model,univariate,implicit)))

[[ex-13.5]]
[example]
.Shell thickness of microscopic spherical particles
====
A practical approach to determining the overlayer thickness of samples of microscopic
spherical particles is to calculate an equivalent planar thickness stem:[T] and
multiply it by a geometrical correction term <<shard>>. An implicit measurement model of
the relationship between the measurand stem:[T] and experimentally measured
independent quantities stem:[A] and stem:[B] is <<cumpson>>

[[eq34]]
[stem]
++++
h(T,A,B) -= e^(-T) - Ae^(-BT) - 1 = 0.
++++

In <<eq34>>, stem:[A] and stem:[B] are the input quantities and stem:[T] is the
output quantity or measurand. It is not possible to re-express stem:[T] explicitly in
terms of stem:[A] and stem:[B]. Using the provisions of <<jcgm102>>, given estimates
stem:[hat A] and stem:[hat B] of stem:[A] of stem:[B], this equation can be solved
numerically for the corresponding estimate stem:[hat T] of stem:[T]. Moreover, the
standard uncertainty stem:[u(hat T)] can be obtained by propagating the standard
uncertainties associated with stem:[hat A] and stem:[hat B].
====

[[cls-13.6]]
=== Implicit multivariate measurement model

A measurement model taking the form

[[eq35]]
[stem]
++++
{: (h_1(Y_1, X_1, ..., X_N) = 0", ", ...", ", h_m(Y_m,X_1,...,X_N) = 0",") :}
++++

implicitly defining the output quantities stem:[Y_1, ..., Y_m] in terms of the input
quantities stem:[X_1, ..., X_N], is termed multivariate and implicit. Typically,
<<eq35>> cannot readily or numerically stably be represented in terms of measurement
functions stem:[f_1, ..., f_m] as in the explicit multivariate case in <<cls-13.4>>.
It is generally solved numerically, typically by using an iterative procedure, to
obtain values of stem:[Y_1, ..., Y_m] corresponding to values of stem:[X_1, ...,
X_N]. This form of model can be handled by the provisions of <<jcgm102>>, which
covers a multivariate generalization of the form of the law of propagation of
uncertainty given in <<jcgm100>> and a ((Monte Carlo method)).

NOTE: It is assumed that the implicit multivariate measurement model (<<eq35>>) can
be solved uniquely for values of stem:[Y_1, ..., Y_m] given values of stem:[X_1, ...,
X_N] close to their estimates.

[[ex-13.6]]
[example]
.Pressures generated by a pressure balance accounting for correlation
====
A generalization of the example of the pressure balance in <<ex2-7.2.1>> is as
follows. For stem:[i = 1, ..., m], let stem:[p_i] denote the generated pressure for
applied mass stem:[m_i] and temperature stem:[t_i], with stem:[A_0], stem:[lambda],
stem:[alpha], stem:[rho_a], stem:[rho_w] and stem:[g] as before. An estimate of each
stem:[p_i] is obtained by solving an equation of the form of <<eq3>> given estimates
of stem:[A_0], stem:[lambda], stem:[alpha], stem:[t_i], stem:[m_i], stem:[rho_a],
stem:[rho_w] and stem:[g]. However, the quantities representing the generated
pressures are not independent because they all depend on the quantities stem:[A_0],
stem:[lambda], stem:[alpha], stem:[rho_a], stem:[rho_w] and stem:[g]. The measurement
is described by a multivariate measurement model in which stem:[A_0], stem:[lambda],
stem:[alpha], stem:[t1, ..., t_m], stem:[m_1, ..., m_m], stem:[rho_a], stem:[rho_w],
stem:[g] are stem:[2m +6] input quantities, stem:[p_1, ..., p_m] are stem:[m] output
quantities, and the measurement model takes the form

[stem%unnumbered]
++++
h_i (Y_1, ..., Y_m, X_1, ..., X_N) = A_0 p_i (1+lambda p_i)[1+ alpha(t_i - t_"ref")] - m_i (1- rho_a/rho_w) g =0, i= 1,...,m,
++++

where stem:[t_"ref"] is a reference temperature, stem:[20 "unitsml(degC)"], say <<lewis>>.
====

[[cls-13.7]]
=== Measurement models involving complex-valued quantities

A complex measurement model involves at least one complex-valued quantity. Complex
models can also be classified as univariate and explicit [of the form of <<eq1>>],
multivariate and explicit [of the form of <<eq31>>], univariate and implicit [of the
form of <<eq33>>], and multivariate and implicit [of the form of <<eq35>>]. For
guidance on working with ((complex numbers)) in the context of measurement models see
<<jcgm102,clause=6.4>>.

[[ex-13.7]]
[example]
.Reflection coefficient measured by a calibrated microwave reflectometer
====
The (complex) reflection coefficient stem:[Gamma] measured by a calibrated microwave
reflectometer, such as an automatic network analyser (ANA), is given by

[[eq36]]
[stem]
++++
Gamma = (aW + b)/(cW + 1),
++++

where stem:[W] is the (complex-valued) quantity representing the uncorrected
reflection coefficient and stem:[a], stem:[b] and stem:[c] are (complex-valued)
calibration coefficients characterizing the reflectometer
<<engen>>, <<kerns>>, <<somlo>>.

There are four (complex-valued) input quantities stem:[a], stem:[b], stem:[c],
stem:[W] and a single (complex-valued) output quantity stem:[Gamma]. <<eq36>> defines
an explicit univariate complex measurement model, the right-hand side constituting
the measurement function.
====
